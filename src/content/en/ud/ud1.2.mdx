---
title: Dutiya bodhi sutta - Upon Awakening (Second)
description: The Buddha contemplates dependent arising in reverse order just after his full awakening.
qualities: radical attention, dependent co-arising
theme: inspiration, wisdom, principle
slug: ud1.2
---

Thus have I heard—At one time, the Blessed One was dwelling at |Uruvelā::name of a town in Magadha, lit. broad banks [uruvelā]| on the bank of the river |Nerañjarā::a river in ancient India, now identified with the modern Phalgu River or its tributary the Niranjana River, lit. the river with pure, clean water [nerañjarā]| at the foot of a goatherd’s banyan tree, having just attained full awakening. Then, for seven days, the Blessed One sat in one posture experiencing the ease of liberation. At the end of those seven days, the Blessed One, emerging from that |perfect peace of mind::stability of mind, stillness of mind [samādhi]|, during the middle watch of the night, thoroughly paid attention to |dependent co-arising::the process of arising together from a cause, chain of causation, dependent origination [paṭiccasamuppāda]| in reverse order:

“When this does not exist, that does not come to be; with the ending of this, that ceases. That is: With the complete fading away and ending of |ignorance::fundamental unawareness or misunderstanding of the true nature of reality, not experientially understanding the four noble truths [avijjā]| comes the ending of |intentional constructs::the constructive activity that shapes each moment of experience, expressed as bodily, verbal, and mental formations; the accumulated conditioning — patterns, tendencies, and habits — produced by prior action [saṅkhārā]|; with the ending of intentional constructs comes the ending of |consciousness::quality of awareness — distinctive knowing that arises in dependence on the meeting of eye and form, ear and sound, nose and odor, tongue and taste, body and tangible object, mind and mind object [viññāṇa]|; with the ending of consciousness comes the ending of |name and form::mentality and materiality—the integrated structure of mental capacities (intention, attention, contact, feeling, perception) and physical form that together constitute and sustain an individual being [nāmarūpa]|; with the ending of name and form comes the ending of the |six sense bases::The six internal sense bases—eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind—are the faculties that enable sensory contact and experience. They are not the physical organs themselves, but the functional conditions that enable consciousness to meet an object [saḷāyatana]|; with the ending of the six sense bases comes the ending of |contact::sense impingement, raw experience, touch [phassa]|; with the ending of contact comes the ending of |felt experience::pleasant, neutral, or painful sensation, feeling, second of the five aggregates [vedanā]|; with the ending of felt experience comes the ending of |craving::wanting, yearning, longing, attachment, lit. thirst [taṇha]|; with the ending of craving comes the ending of |clinging::grasping, acquiring, appropriating, taking possession, identifying; fuel for fire; lit. taking near [upādāna]|; with the ending of clinging comes the ending of |existence::being, becoming; continued conditional existence, the karmically conditioned mode of being that leads to future rebirth [bhava]|; with the ending of existence comes the ending of |birth::rebirth, conception, coming into existence [jāti]|; with the ending of birth, aging and death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair cease. Thus is the ending of this whole mass of suffering.”

Then, understanding the significance of this, the Blessed One at that time expressed this inspired utterance:

“When the nature of things becomes evident,
to the |sage::Brāhmaṇa, a title used for an Arahant, an awakened being [brāhmaṇa]| meditating |with continuous effort::ardent, zealous, with energy, with application; lit. burning [ātāpī]|;
then all his |doubts::uncertainties, perplexities, hesitations—especially in relation to faith, truth, or decision-making on the path [kaṅkhā]| completely disappear,
as he experiences the |wearing away::exhaustion, depletion, gradual destruction [khaya]| of causes.”
