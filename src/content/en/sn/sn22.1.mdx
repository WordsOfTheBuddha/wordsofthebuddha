Thus have I heard—At one time, the Blessed One was dwelling among the |Bhaggans::name of the people of Bhagga [bhaggā]| in |Crocodile Mountain::name of the capital city of Bhagga [susumāragira]|, in the Deer Park at Bhesakaḷā Grove.

Then the householder |Nakulapitā::name of a householder; lit. Nakula's father [nakulapitā]| approached the Blessed One; having approached, he paid homage to the Blessed One and sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, the householder Nakulapitā said to the Blessed One:

“I am now, venerable sir, aged, old, elderly, advanced in years, and have reached the final stage of life; my body is afflicted, and I am frequently sick. Moreover, venerable sir, I rarely get to see the Blessed One and the esteemed bhikkhus. May the Blessed One exhort me, venerable sir; may the Blessed One instruct me, venerable sir, for my long-term welfare and happiness.”

“So it is, householder, so it is, householder. For this body of yours, householder, is afflicted, |frail::fragile; lit. become an egg [aṇḍabhūta]|, and encumbered. For one carrying around this body, householder, to claim even a moment of |health::wellness, freedom from disease [ārogya]|—what is that if not |foolishness::ignorance; lit. fool state [bālya]|?

Therefore householder, you should train yourself thus: ‘Even though my body is afflicted, my mind will not be afflicted.’ Thus should you train yourself, householder.

Then the householder Nakulapitā, being delighted and pleased with the Blessed One’s words, rose from his seat, paid homage to the Blessed One, keeping him on his right, and approached the venerable Sāriputta. Having approached, he paid homage to the venerable Sāriputta and sat down to one side. As the householder Nakulapitā was sitting to one side, the venerable Sāriputta said to him:

“Your faculties are clear, householder; your facial complexion is pure and bright. Did you get to hear a Dhamma talk in the presence of the Blessed One today?”

“How could it be otherwise, venerable sir? Just now, venerable sir, I was sprinkled with the nectar of a Dhamma talk by the Blessed One.”

“And in what way, householder, were you sprinkled with the nectar of a Dhamma talk by the Blessed One?”

“Here, venerable sir, I approached the Blessed One; having approached, I paid homage to the Blessed One and sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, venerable sir, I said to the Blessed One: ‘I am now, venerable sir, aged, old, elderly, advanced in years, and have reached the final stage of life; my body is afflicted, and I am frequently sick. Moreover, venerable sir, I rarely get to see the Blessed One and the esteemed bhikkhus. May the Blessed One exhort me, venerable sir; may the Blessed One instruct me, venerable sir, for my long-term welfare and happiness.’ When this was said, venerable sir, the Blessed One said to me: ‘So it is, householder, so it is, householder. For this body of yours, householder, is afflicted, frail, and encumbered. For one carrying around this body, householder, to claim even a moment of health—what is that if not foolishness? Therefore, householder, you should train yourself thus: “Even though my body is afflicted, my mind will not be afflicted.” Thus should you train yourself, householder.’ In this way, venerable sir, I was sprinkled with the nectar of a Dhamma talk by the Blessed One.”

“But did it not occur to you, householder, to question the Blessed One further: ‘In what way, venerable sir, is one afflicted in body and afflicted in mind, and in what way is one afflicted in body but not afflicted in mind?’”

“Even from far away, venerable sir, we would come to venerable Sāriputta to learn the meaning of this statement. It would be good if venerable Sāriputta himself would clarify the meaning of this statement.”

“Well then, householder, listen to this and pay close attention; I will speak.”

“Yes, venerable sir,” the householder Nakulapitā replied to the venerable Sāriputta. The venerable Sāriputta said this:

#### Afflicted in Body and Afflicted in Mind

“And how, householder, is one afflicted in body and afflicted in mind? Here, householder, an |uninstructed::uninitiated, untaught, untrained [assutavant]| ordinary person, |who has no regard for::lit. who has not seen [adassāvī]| the Noble Ones and is unskilled and untrained in the |Dhamma::teachings of the Buddha that point to the nature of reality, the ultimate truth [dhamma]| of the Noble Ones; who has no regard for persons of integrity and is unskilled and untrained in the Dhamma of persons of integrity, perceives |form::materiality, material existence, experience of the material world, i.e. encompassing both one’s body and external objects, whether near or far, gross or subtle, deficient or refined; first of the five aggregates [rūpa]| as self, or self as possessing form, or form as being in self, or self as being in form. He is obsessed with the thought, ‘I am form, form is mine.’ As he is obsessed with the thought, ‘I am form, form is mine,’ that form of his then changes and alters. With the change and alteration of form, there arise in him sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair.

He perceives |feeling::pleasant, neutral, or painful sensation, the experience felt on contact through eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind; second of the five aggregates [vedanā]| as self, or self as possessing feeling, or feeling as being in self, or self as being in feeling. He is obsessed with the thought, ‘I am feeling, feeling is mine.’ As he is obsessed with the thought, ‘I am feeling, feeling is mine,’ that feeling of his then changes and alters. With the change and alteration of feeling, there arise in him sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair.

He perceives |perception::The mental process of recognizing and giving meaning to experience. It marks sensory information by signs, labels, or associations drawn from memory and the field of contact. Perception shapes how one experiences the world; third of the five aggregates [sañña]| as self, or self as possessing perception, or perception as being in self, or self as being in perception. He is obsessed with the thought, ‘I am perception, perception is mine.’ As he is obsessed with the thought, ‘I am perception, perception is mine,’ that perception of his then changes and alters. With the change and alteration of perception, there arise in him sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair.

He perceives |intentional constructs::the constructive activity that shapes each moment of experience, expressed as bodily, verbal, and mental formations; the accumulated conditioning — patterns, tendencies, and habits — produced by prior action [saṅkhārā]| as self, or self as possessing intentional constructs, or intentional constructs as being in self, or self as being in intentional constructs. He is obsessed with the thought, ‘I am intentional constructs, intentional constructs are mine.’ As he is obsessed with the thought, ‘I am intentional constructs, intentional constructs are mine,’ those intentional constructs of his then change and alter. With the change and alteration of intentional constructs, there arise in him sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair.

He perceives |consciousness::quality of awareness — distinctive knowing that arises in dependence on the meeting of eye and form, ear and sound, nose and odor, tongue and taste, body and tangible object, mind and mind object [viññāṇa]| as self, or self as possessing consciousness, or consciousness as being in self, or self as being in consciousness. He is obsessed with the thought, ‘I am consciousness, consciousness is mine.’ As he is obsessed with the thought, ‘I am consciousness, consciousness is mine,’ that consciousness of his then changes and alters. With the change and alteration of consciousness, there arise in him sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair.

That is how, householder, one is afflicted in body and afflicted in mind.

#### Afflicted in Body but Not Afflicted in Mind

And how, householder, is one afflicted in body but not afflicted in mind? Here, householder, the learned disciple of the Noble Ones, who has regard for the Noble Ones and is skilled and well-trained in the Dhamma of the Noble Ones; who has regard for persons of integrity and is skilled and well-trained in the Dhamma of persons of integrity, does not perceive form as self, nor self as possessing form, nor form as being in self, nor self as being in form. He is not obsessed with the thought, ‘I am form, form is mine.’ Because he is not obsessed with the thought, ‘I am form, form is mine,’ when that form of his changes and alters, there do not arise in him sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair.

He does not perceive feeling as self, nor self as possessing feeling, nor feeling as being in self, nor self as being in feeling. He is not obsessed with the thought, ‘I am feeling, feeling is mine.’ Because he is not obsessed with the thought, ‘I am feeling, feeling is mine,’ when that feeling of his changes and alters, there do not arise in him sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair.

He does not perceive perception as self, nor self as possessing perception, nor perception as being in self, nor self as being in perception. He is not obsessed with the thought, ‘I am perception, perception is mine.’ Because he is not obsessed with the thought, ‘I am perception, perception is mine,’ when that perception of his changes and alters, there do not arise in him sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair.

He does not perceive intentional constructs as self, nor self as possessing intentional constructs, nor intentional constructs as being in self, nor self as being in intentional constructs. He is not obsessed with the thought, ‘I am intentional constructs, intentional constructs are mine.’ Because he is not obsessed with the thought, ‘I am intentional constructs, intentional constructs are mine,’ when those intentional constructs of his change and alter, there do not arise in him sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair.

He does not perceive consciousness as self, nor self as possessing consciousness, nor consciousness as being in self, nor self as being in consciousness. He is not obsessed with the thought, ‘I am consciousness, consciousness is mine.’ Because he is not obsessed with the thought, ‘I am consciousness, consciousness is mine,’ when that consciousness of his changes and alters, there do not arise in him sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair.

That is how, householder, one is afflicted in body but not afflicted in mind.”

The venerable Sāriputta said this. The householder Nakulapitā  was delighted and pleased with the words of venerable Sāriputta.
