<!-- @segment an5.194:1.1 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Vesālī, at the Great Wood, in the hall with the peaked roof.

<!-- @segment an5.194:1.2 -->

Now at that time the brahmin Kāraṇapālī was working for the Licchavis.

<!-- @segment an5.194:1.3 -->

He saw the brahmin Piṅgiyānī coming off in the distance

<!-- @segment an5.194:1.4 -->

and said to him,

<!-- @segment an5.194:2.1 -->

“So, Piṅgiyānī, where are you coming from in the middle of the day?”

<!-- @segment an5.194:2.2 -->

“I’m coming, mister, from the presence of the ascetic Gotama.”

<!-- @segment an5.194:2.3 -->

“What do you think of the ascetic Gotama’s lucidity of wisdom? Do you think he’s astute?”

<!-- @segment an5.194:2.4 -->

“Mister, who am I to judge the ascetic Gotama’s lucidity of wisdom?

<!-- @segment an5.194:2.5 -->

You’d really have to be on the same level to judge his lucidity of wisdom.”

<!-- @segment an5.194:2.6 -->

“Mister Piṅgiyānī praises the ascetic Gotama with high praise indeed.”

<!-- @segment an5.194:2.7 -->

“Who am I to praise the ascetic Gotama?

<!-- @segment an5.194:2.8 -->

He is praised by the praised as the first among gods and humans.”

<!-- @segment an5.194:2.9 -->

“But for what reason are you so devoted to the ascetic Gotama?”

<!-- @segment an5.194:3.1 -->

“Suppose a person was completely satisfied by the best tasting food. They wouldn’t be attracted to anything that tasted inferior.

<!-- @segment an5.194:3.2 -->

In the same way, when you hear the worthy Gotama’s teaching—

<!-- @segment an5.194:3.3 -->

whatever it may be, whether statements, mixed prose & verse, discussions, or amazing stories—

<!-- @segment an5.194:3.4 -->

then you’re not attracted to the doctrines of the various ascetics and brahmins.

<!-- @segment an5.194:4.1 -->

Suppose a person who was weak with hunger was to obtain a honey-cake.

<!-- @segment an5.194:4.2 -->

Wherever they taste it, they would enjoy a sweet, delectable flavor.

<!-- @segment an5.194:4.3 -->

In the same way, when you hear the worthy Gotama’s teaching—

<!-- @segment an5.194:4.4 -->

whatever it may be, whether statements, mixed prose & verse, discussions, or amazing stories—

<!-- @segment an5.194:4.5 -->

then you get a sense of uplift, a confidence of the heart.

<!-- @segment an5.194:5.1 -->

Suppose a person were to obtain a piece of sandalwood,

<!-- @segment an5.194:5.2 -->

whether yellow or red.

<!-- @segment an5.194:5.3 -->

Wherever they smelled it—

<!-- @segment an5.194:5.4 -->

whether at the root, the middle, or the top—

<!-- @segment an5.194:5.5 -->

they’d enjoy an delectable fragrance.

<!-- @segment an5.194:5.6 -->

In the same way, when you hear the worthy Gotama’s teaching—

<!-- @segment an5.194:5.7 -->

whatever it may be, whether statements, mixed prose & verse, discussions, or amazing stories—

<!-- @segment an5.194:5.8 -->

then you become filled with joy and happiness.

<!-- @segment an5.194:6.1 -->

Suppose there was a person who was sick, suffering, gravely ill.

<!-- @segment an5.194:6.2 -->

A good doctor would cure them on the spot.

<!-- @segment an5.194:6.3 -->

In the same way, when you hear the worthy Gotama’s teaching—

<!-- @segment an5.194:6.4 -->

whatever it may be, whether statements, mixed prose & verse, discussions, or amazing stories—

<!-- @segment an5.194:6.5 -->

then you make an end of sorrow, lamentation, pain, sadness, and distress.

<!-- @segment an5.194:7.1 -->

Suppose there was a lotus pond with clear, sweet, cool water, clean, with smooth banks, delightful.

<!-- @segment an5.194:7.2 -->

Then along comes a person struggling in the oppressive heat, weary, thirsty, and parched.

<!-- @segment an5.194:7.3 -->

They’d plunge into the lotus pond to bathe and drink. And all their stress, weariness, and heat exhaustion would die down.

<!-- @segment an5.194:7.4 -->

In the same way, when you hear the worthy Gotama’s teaching—

<!-- @segment an5.194:7.5 -->

whatever it may be, whether statements, mixed prose & verse, discussions, or amazing stories—

<!-- @segment an5.194:7.6 -->

then all your stress, weariness, and exhaustion die down.”

<!-- @segment an5.194:8.1 -->

When this was said, the brahmin Kāraṇapālī rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, knelt on his right knee, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha, and expressed this heartfelt sentiment three times:

<!-- @segment an5.194:9.1 -->

“Homage to him, the blessed one, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha!

<!-- @segment an5.194:10.1 -->

Homage to him, the blessed one, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha!

<!-- @segment an5.194:11.1 -->

Homage to him, the blessed one, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha!

<!-- @segment an5.194:12.1 -->

Excellent, worthy Piṅgiyānī! Excellent!

<!-- @segment an5.194:12.2 -->

As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark so people with clear eyes can see what’s there, the worthy Piṅgiyānī has made the teaching clear in many ways.

<!-- @segment an5.194:12.3 -->

I go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha.

<!-- @segment an5.194:12.4 -->

From this day forth, may the worthy Piṅgiyānī remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”
