<!-- @segment an2.32:1.0 -->

32

<!-- @segment an2.32:1.1 -->

“Mendicants, I will teach you the level of the untrue person and the level of the true person.

<!-- @segment an2.32:1.2 -->

Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.”

<!-- @segment an2.32:1.3 -->

“Yes, sir,” they replied.

<!-- @segment an2.32:1.4 -->

The Buddha said this:

<!-- @segment an2.32:2.1 -->

“What is the level of the untrue person?

<!-- @segment an2.32:2.2 -->

The untrue person is ungrateful and thankless,

<!-- @segment an2.32:2.3 -->

for the wicked only know how to be ungrateful and thankless.

<!-- @segment an2.32:2.4 -->

It is totally the level of an untrue person to be ungrateful and thankless.

<!-- @segment an2.32:2.5 -->

The true person is grateful and thankful,

<!-- @segment an2.32:2.6 -->

for the virtuous only know how to be grateful and thankful.

<!-- @segment an2.32:2.7 -->

It is totally the level of a true person to be grateful and thankful.”

<!-- @segment an2.33:1.0 -->

33

<!-- @segment an2.33:1.1 -->

“Mendicants, I say that these two individuals cannot easily be repaid.

<!-- @segment an2.33:1.2 -->

What two?

<!-- @segment an2.33:1.3 -->

Mother and father.

<!-- @segment an2.33:1.4 -->

You would not have done enough to repay your mother and father even if you were to carry your mother around on one shoulder and your father on the other, and if you lived like this for a hundred years, and if you were to anoint, massage, bathe, and rub them;

<!-- @segment an2.33:1.5 -->

and even if they were to defecate and urinate right there.

<!-- @segment an2.33:1.6 -->

Even if you were to establish your mother and father as supreme monarchs of this great earth, abounding in the seven treasures, you would still not have done enough to repay them.

<!-- @segment an2.33:1.7 -->

Why is that?

<!-- @segment an2.33:1.8 -->

Parents are very helpful to their children: they raise them, nurture them, and show them the world.

<!-- @segment an2.33:1.9 -->

But you have done enough, more than enough, to repay them if you encourage, settle, and ground unfaithful parents in faith, unethical parents in ethical conduct, stingy parents in generosity, or ignorant parents in wisdom.”

<!-- @segment an2.34:1.0 -->

34

<!-- @segment an2.34:1.1 -->

Then a certain brahmin went up to the Buddha, and exchanged greetings with him. When the greetings and polite conversation were over, he sat down to one side and said to the Buddha,

<!-- @segment an2.34:1.2 -->

“What does the worthy Gotama teach? What does he explain?”

<!-- @segment an2.34:1.3 -->

“Brahmin, I teach action and inaction.”

<!-- @segment an2.34:1.4 -->

“But in what way does the worthy Gotama teach action and inaction?”

<!-- @segment an2.34:2.1 -->

“I teach inaction regarding bad bodily, verbal, and mental conduct, and the many kinds of unskillful things.

<!-- @segment an2.34:2.2 -->

I teach action regarding good bodily, verbal, and mental conduct, and the many kinds of skillful things.

<!-- @segment an2.34:2.3 -->

This is the kind of action and inaction that I teach.”

<!-- @segment an2.34:3.1 -->

“Excellent, worthy Gotama! … From this day forth, may the worthy Gotama remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

<!-- @segment an2.35:1.0 -->

35

<!-- @segment an2.35:1.1 -->

Then the householder Anāthapiṇḍika went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to the Buddha,

<!-- @segment an2.35:1.2 -->

“How many in the world are worthy of a religious donation? And where should a gift be given?”

<!-- @segment an2.35:1.3 -->

“Householder, there are two in the world who are worthy of a religious donation:

<!-- @segment an2.35:1.4 -->

the trainee and the adept.

<!-- @segment an2.35:1.5 -->

These two in the world are worthy of a religious donation, and that’s where you should give a gift.”

<!-- @segment an2.35:2.1 -->

That is what the Buddha said.

<!-- @segment an2.35:2.2 -->

Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say:

<!-- @segment an2.35:3.1 -->

“In this world, the trainee and the adept,

<!-- @segment an2.35:3.2 -->

are worthy of offerings dedicated to the gods.

<!-- @segment an2.35:3.3 -->

They are sincere in body,

<!-- @segment an2.35:3.4 -->

speech, and mind.

<!-- @segment an2.35:3.5 -->

This is the field for sponsors of sacrifice—

<!-- @segment an2.35:3.6 -->

what’s given here is very fruitful.”

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.0 -->

36

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery.

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.3 -->

Now at that time Venerable Sāriputta was staying near Sāvatthī in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery.

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.4 -->

There Sāriputta addressed the mendicants:

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.5 -->

“Reverends, mendicants!”

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.6 -->

“Reverend,” they replied.

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.7 -->

Sāriputta said this:

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.8 -->

“I will teach you about an individual fettered internally and one fettered externally.

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.9 -->

Listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.”

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.10 -->

“Yes, reverend,” they replied.

<!-- @segment an2.36:1.11 -->

Sāriputta said this:

<!-- @segment an2.36:2.1 -->

“Who is an individual fettered internally?

<!-- @segment an2.36:2.2 -->

It’s a mendicant who is ethical, restrained in the monastic code, conducting themselves well and resorting for alms in suitable places. Seeing danger in the slightest fault, they keep the rules they’ve undertaken.

<!-- @segment an2.36:2.3 -->

When their body breaks up, after death, they’re reborn in one of the orders of gods.

<!-- @segment an2.36:2.4 -->

When they pass away from there, they’re a returner, who comes back to this place.

<!-- @segment an2.36:2.5 -->

This is called an individual who is fettered internally, a returner, who comes back to this place.

<!-- @segment an2.36:3.1 -->

Who is an individual fettered externally?

<!-- @segment an2.36:3.2 -->

It’s a mendicant who is ethical, restrained in the monastic code, conducting themselves well and resorting for alms in suitable places. Seeing danger in the slightest fault, they keep the rules they’ve undertaken.

<!-- @segment an2.36:3.3 -->

They enter and remain in a certain peaceful state of freed mind.

<!-- @segment an2.36:3.4 -->

When their body breaks up, after death, they’re reborn in one of the orders of gods.

<!-- @segment an2.36:3.5 -->

When they pass away from there, they’re a non-returner, not coming back to this place.

<!-- @segment an2.36:3.6 -->

This is called an individual who is fettered externally, a non-returner, who does not come back to this place.

<!-- @segment an2.36:4.1 -->

Furthermore, a mendicant is ethical … they keep the rules they’ve undertaken.

<!-- @segment an2.36:4.2 -->

They simply practice for disillusionment, dispassion, and cessation regarding sensual pleasures.

<!-- @segment an2.36:4.3 -->

They simply practice for disillusionment, dispassion, and cessation regarding future lives.

<!-- @segment an2.36:4.4 -->

They practice for the ending of craving.

<!-- @segment an2.36:4.5 -->

They practice for the ending of greed.

<!-- @segment an2.36:4.6 -->

When their body breaks up, after death, they are reborn in one of the orders of gods.

<!-- @segment an2.36:4.7 -->

When they pass away from there, they are non-returners, not coming back to this place.

<!-- @segment an2.36:4.8 -->

This is called an individual who is fettered externally, a non-returner, who does not come back to this place.”

<!-- @segment an2.36:5.1 -->

Then several peaceful-minded deities went up to the Buddha, bowed, stood to one side, and said to the Buddha,

<!-- @segment an2.36:5.2 -->

“Sir, Venerable Sāriputta is in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery, where he is teaching the mendicants about an individual with internal fetters and one with external fetters.

<!-- @segment an2.36:5.3 -->

The assembly is overjoyed!

<!-- @segment an2.36:5.4 -->

Sir, please go to Venerable Sāriputta out of sympathy.”

<!-- @segment an2.36:5.5 -->

The Buddha consented with silence.

<!-- @segment an2.36:5.6 -->

Then the Buddha, as easily as a strong person would extend or contract their arm, vanished from Jeta’s Grove and reappeared in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery, in front of Sāriputta.

<!-- @segment an2.36:5.7 -->

He sat on the seat spread out.

<!-- @segment an2.36:5.8 -->

Sāriputta bowed to the Buddha and sat down to one side.

<!-- @segment an2.36:5.9 -->

The Buddha said to him:

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.1 -->

“Just now, Sāriputta, several peaceful-minded deities came up to me, bowed, and stood to one side. Those deities said to me:

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.2 -->

‘Sir, Venerable Sāriputta is in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery, where he is teaching the mendicants about an individual with internal fetters and one with external fetters.

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.3 -->

The assembly is overjoyed!

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.4 -->

Sir, please go to Venerable Sāriputta out of sympathy.’

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.5 -->

Those deities, though they number ten, twenty, thirty, forty, fifty, or sixty, can stand on the point of a needle without bumping up against each other.

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.6 -->

Sāriputta, you might think:

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.7 -->

‘Surely those deities, since so many of them can stand on the point of a needle without bumping up against each other, must have developed their minds in that place.’

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.8 -->

But you should not see it like this.

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.9 -->

It was right here that those deities developed their minds.

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.10 -->

So you should train like this:

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.11 -->

‘We shall have peaceful faculties and peaceful minds.’

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.12 -->

That’s how you should train.

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.13 -->

When your faculties and mind are peaceful, your acts of body, speech, and mind will be peaceful, thinking:

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.14 -->

‘We shall present the gift of peace to our spiritual companions.’

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.15 -->

That’s how you should train.

<!-- @segment an2.36:6.16 -->

Those wanderers of other religions, Sāriputta, who have not heard this exposition of the teaching are lost.”

<!-- @segment an2.37:1.0 -->

37

<!-- @segment an2.37:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment an2.37:1.2 -->

At one time Venerable Mahākaccāna was staying at Varaṇā, on the bank of the Kaddama Lake.

<!-- @segment an2.37:1.3 -->

Then the brahmin Ārāmadaṇḍa went up to Mahākaccāna, and exchanged greetings with him.

<!-- @segment an2.37:1.4 -->

When the greetings and polite conversation were over, he sat down to one side and said to Mahākaccāna:

<!-- @segment an2.37:1.5 -->

“What is the cause, worthy Kaccāna, what is the reason why aristocrats fight with aristocrats, brahmins fight with brahmins, and householders fight with householders?”

<!-- @segment an2.37:1.6 -->

“It is because of their insistence on sensual desire, their shackles, avarice, and attachment, that aristocrats fight with aristocrats, brahmins fight with brahmins, and householders fight with householders.”

<!-- @segment an2.37:2.1 -->

“What is the cause, worthy Kaccāna, what is the reason why ascetics fight with ascetics?”

<!-- @segment an2.37:2.2 -->

“It is because of their insistence on views, their shackles, avarice, and attachment, that ascetics fight with ascetics.”

<!-- @segment an2.37:3.1 -->

“Worthy Kaccāna, is there anyone in the world who has gone beyond the insistence on sensual desire and the insistence on views?”

<!-- @segment an2.37:3.2 -->

“There is, brahmin.”

<!-- @segment an2.37:4.1 -->

“Who in the world has gone beyond the insistence on sensual desire and the insistence on views?”

<!-- @segment an2.37:4.2 -->

“In the eastern lands there is a city named Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment an2.37:4.3 -->

There the Blessed One is now staying, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha.

<!-- @segment an2.37:4.4 -->

He, brahmin, has gone beyond the insistence on sensual desire and the insistence on views.”

<!-- @segment an2.37:5.1 -->

When this was said, the brahmin Ārāmadaṇḍa rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, knelt on his right knee, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha, and expressed this heartfelt sentiment three times:

<!-- @segment an2.37:6.1 -->

“Homage to him, the blessed one, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha!

<!-- @segment an2.37:7.1 -->

Homage to him, the blessed one, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha!

<!-- @segment an2.37:8.1 -->

Homage to him, the blessed one, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha!

<!-- @segment an2.37:9.1 -->

He who has gone beyond the insistence on sensual desire and the insistence on views.

<!-- @segment an2.37:10.1 -->

Excellent, worthy Kaccāna! Excellent!

<!-- @segment an2.37:10.2 -->

As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark so people with clear eyes can see what’s there, the worthy Kaccāna has made the teaching clear in many ways.

<!-- @segment an2.37:10.3 -->

I go for refuge to the worthy Gotama, to the teaching, and to the mendicant Saṅgha.

<!-- @segment an2.37:10.4 -->

From this day forth, may the worthy Kaccāna remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

<!-- @segment an2.38:1.0 -->

38

<!-- @segment an2.38:1.1 -->

At one time Venerable Mahākaccāna was staying near Madhurā, in the Nut Grass Grove.

<!-- @segment an2.38:1.2 -->

Then the brahmin Kandarāyana went up to Mahākaccāna, and exchanged greetings with him … He sat down to one side and said to Mahākaccāna:

<!-- @segment an2.38:1.3 -->

“I have heard, worthy Kaccāna, that the ascetic Kaccāna doesn’t bow to old brahmins, the elderly and senior, who are advanced in years and have reached the final stage of life; nor does he rise in their presence or offer them a seat.

<!-- @segment an2.38:1.4 -->

And this is indeed the case,

<!-- @segment an2.38:1.5 -->

for the ascetic Kaccāna does not bow to old brahmins, elderly and senior, who are advanced in years and have reached the final stage of life; nor does he rise in their presence or offer them a seat.

<!-- @segment an2.38:1.6 -->

This is not appropriate, worthy Kaccāna.”

<!-- @segment an2.38:2.1 -->

“There is the stage of an elder and the stage of youth as explained by the Blessed One, who knows and sees, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha.

<!-- @segment an2.38:2.2 -->

If an elder, though eighty, ninety, or a hundred years old, still dwells in the midst of sensual pleasures, enjoying them, consumed by thoughts of them, burning with fever for them, and eagerly seeking more,

<!-- @segment an2.38:2.3 -->

they are reckoned as a child, not a senior.

<!-- @segment an2.38:2.4 -->

If a youth, young, with pristine black hair, blessed with youth, in the prime of life,

<!-- @segment an2.38:2.5 -->

does not dwell in the midst of sensual pleasures, enjoying them, consumed by thoughts of them, burning with fever for them, and eagerly seeking more,

<!-- @segment an2.38:2.6 -->

they are reckoned as astute, a senior.”

<!-- @segment an2.38:3.1 -->

When this was said, the brahmin Kandarāyana rose from his seat, placed his robe over one shoulder, and bowed with his head at the feet of the young mendicants, saying,

<!-- @segment an2.38:3.2 -->

“The good fellows are elders, at the stage of the elder;

<!-- @segment an2.38:3.3 -->

we are youths, at the stage of youth.

<!-- @segment an2.38:4.1 -->

Excellent, worthy Kaccāna! … From this day forth, may the worthy Kaccāna remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

<!-- @segment an2.39:1.0 -->

39

<!-- @segment an2.39:1.1 -->

“At a time when bandits are strong, kings are weak.

<!-- @segment an2.39:1.2 -->

Then the king is not at ease when going out or coming back or when touring the provinces.

<!-- @segment an2.39:1.3 -->

The brahmins and householders, likewise, are not at ease when going out or coming back, or when inspecting their external business activities.

<!-- @segment an2.39:1.4 -->

In the same way, at a time when bad mendicants are strong, good-hearted mendicants are weak.

<!-- @segment an2.39:1.5 -->

Then the good-hearted mendicants continually adhere to silence in the midst of the Saṅgha, or they stay in the borderlands.

<!-- @segment an2.39:1.6 -->

This is for the detriment and suffering of the people, against the people, for the harm, detriment, and suffering of gods and humans.

<!-- @segment an2.39:2.1 -->

At a time when kings are strong, bandits are weak.

<!-- @segment an2.39:2.2 -->

Then the king is at ease when going out or coming back or when inspecting the provinces.

<!-- @segment an2.39:2.3 -->

The brahmins and householders, likewise, are at ease when going out or coming back, or when inspecting their external business activities.

<!-- @segment an2.39:2.4 -->

In the same way, at a time when good-hearted mendicants are strong, bad mendicants are weak.

<!-- @segment an2.39:2.5 -->

Then the bad mendicants continually adhere to silence in the midst of the Saṅgha, or they leave for some place or other.

<!-- @segment an2.39:2.6 -->

This is for the welfare and happiness of the people, for the people, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans.”

<!-- @segment an2.40:1.0 -->

40

<!-- @segment an2.40:1.1 -->

“Mendicants, I don’t praise wrong practice for these two, for laypeople and renunciates.

<!-- @segment an2.40:1.2 -->

Because of wrong practice, neither laypeople nor renunciates succeed in completing the system of the skillful teaching.

<!-- @segment an2.40:2.1 -->

I praise right practice for these two, for laypeople and renunciates.

<!-- @segment an2.40:2.2 -->

Because of right practice, both laypeople and renunciates succeed in completing the system of the skillful teaching.”

<!-- @segment an2.41:1.0 -->

41

<!-- @segment an2.41:1.1 -->

“Mendicants, by memorizing the discourses incorrectly, taking only a semblance of the phrasing, some mendicants shut out the meaning and the teaching. They act for the detriment and suffering of the people, against the people, for the harm, detriment, and suffering of gods and humans.

<!-- @segment an2.41:1.2 -->

They brim with much wickedness and make the true teaching disappear.

<!-- @segment an2.41:2.1 -->

But by memorizing the discourses well, not taking only a semblance of the phrasing, some mendicants conform to the meaning and the teaching. They act for the welfare and happiness of the people, for the people, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans.

<!-- @segment an2.41:2.2 -->

They brim with much merit and make the true teaching continue.”
