<!-- @segment an9.8:1.1 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, on the Vulture’s Peak Mountain.

<!-- @segment an9.8:1.2 -->

Then the wanderer Sajjha went up to the Buddha, and exchanged greetings with him.

<!-- @segment an9.8:1.3 -->

When the greetings and polite conversation were over, he sat down to one side and said to the Buddha:

<!-- @segment an9.8:2.1 -->

“Sir, this one time the Buddha was staying right here in Rājagaha, the Mountainfold.

<!-- @segment an9.8:2.2 -->

There I heard and learned this in the presence of the Buddha:

<!-- @segment an9.8:2.3 -->

‘A mendicant who is perfected—with defilements ended, who has completed the spiritual journey, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their heart’s goal, utterly ended the fetter of continued existence, and is rightly freed through enlightenment—can’t transgress in five respects.

<!-- @segment an9.8:2.4 -->

A mendicant with defilements ended can’t deliberately take the life of a living creature, take something with the intention to steal, have sex, tell a deliberate lie, or store up goods for their own enjoyment like they did as a layperson.’

<!-- @segment an9.8:2.5 -->

I trust I properly heard, learned, applied the mind, and remembered that from the Buddha?”

<!-- @segment an9.8:3.1 -->

“Indeed, Sajjha, you properly heard, learned, applied the mind, and remembered that.

<!-- @segment an9.8:3.2 -->

In the past, as today, I say this:

<!-- @segment an9.8:3.3 -->

‘A mendicant who is perfected—with defilements ended, who has completed the spiritual journey, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their heart’s goal, utterly ended the fetter of continued existence, and is rightly freed through enlightenment—can’t transgress in nine respects.

<!-- @segment an9.8:3.4 -->

A mendicant with defilements ended can’t deliberately kill a living creature, take something with the intention to steal, have sex, tell a deliberate lie, or store up goods for their own enjoyment like they used to as a layperson. And they can’t abandon the Buddha, the teaching, the Saṅgha, or the training.’

<!-- @segment an9.8:3.5 -->

In the past, as today, I say this:

<!-- @segment an9.8:3.6 -->

‘A mendicant who is perfected—with defilements ended, who has completed the spiritual journey, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their heart’s goal, utterly ended the fetter of continued existence, and is rightly freed through enlightenment—can’t transgress in these nine respects.’”
