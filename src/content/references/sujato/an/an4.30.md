<!-- @segment an4.30:1.1 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, on the Vulture’s Peak Mountain.

<!-- @segment an4.30:1.2 -->

Now at that time several very well-known wanderers were residing in the monastery of the wanderers on the bank of the Serpentine river. They included Annabhāra, Varadhara, Sakuludāyī, and other very well-known wanderers.

<!-- @segment an4.30:1.3 -->

Then in the late afternoon, the Buddha came out of retreat and went to the wanderer’s monastery on the banks of the Serpentine river, He sat down on the seat spread out, and said to the wanderers:

<!-- @segment an4.30:2.1 -->

“Wanderers, these four footprints of the Dhamma are primordial, long-standing, traditional, and ancient. They are uncorrupted, as they have been since the beginning. They’re not being corrupted now nor will they be. Sensible ascetics and brahmins don’t look down on them.

<!-- @segment an4.30:2.2 -->

What four?

<!-- @segment an4.30:2.3 -->

Contentment …

<!-- @segment an4.30:2.4 -->

Good will … Right mindfulness … Right immersion …

<!-- @segment an4.30:2.5 -->

These four footprints of the Dhamma are primordial, long-standing, traditional, and ancient. They are uncorrupted, as they have been since the beginning. They’re not being corrupted now nor will they be. Sensible ascetics and brahmins don’t look down on them.

<!-- @segment an4.30:3.1 -->

Wanderers, if someone should say:

<!-- @segment an4.30:3.2 -->

‘I’ll deny this Dhamma footprint of contentment, and describe a true ascetic or brahmin who covets sensual pleasures with acute lust.’ Then I’d say to them:

<!-- @segment an4.30:3.3 -->

‘Let them come, speak, and discuss. We’ll see how powerful they are.’

<!-- @segment an4.30:3.4 -->

It is quite impossible to deny this Dhamma footprint of contentment, and point out a true ascetic or brahmin who covets sensual pleasures with acute lust.

<!-- @segment an4.30:4.1 -->

If someone should say:

<!-- @segment an4.30:4.2 -->

‘I’ll deny this Dhamma footprint of good will, and describe a true ascetic or brahmin who has ill will and malicious intent.’ Then I’d say to them:

<!-- @segment an4.30:4.3 -->

‘Let them come, speak, and discuss. We’ll see how powerful they are.’

<!-- @segment an4.30:4.4 -->

It is quite impossible to deny this Dhamma footprint of good will, and point out a true ascetic or brahmin who has ill will and malicious intent.

<!-- @segment an4.30:5.1 -->

If someone should say:

<!-- @segment an4.30:5.2 -->

‘I’ll deny this Dhamma footprint of right mindfulness, and describe a true ascetic or brahmin who is unmindful, with no situational awareness.’ Then I’d say to them:

<!-- @segment an4.30:5.3 -->

‘Let them come, speak, and discuss. We’ll see how powerful they are.’

<!-- @segment an4.30:5.4 -->

It is quite impossible to deny this Dhamma footprint of right mindfulness, and point out a true ascetic or brahmin who is unmindful, with no situational awareness.

<!-- @segment an4.30:6.1 -->

If someone should say:

<!-- @segment an4.30:6.2 -->

‘I’ll deny this Dhamma footprint of right immersion, and describe a true ascetic or brahmin who is scattered, with straying mind.’ Then I’d say to them:

<!-- @segment an4.30:6.3 -->

‘Let them come, speak, and discuss. We’ll see how powerful they are.’

<!-- @segment an4.30:6.4 -->

It is quite impossible to deny this Dhamma footprint of right immersion, and point out a true ascetic or brahmin who is scattered, with straying mind.

<!-- @segment an4.30:7.1 -->

If anyone imagines they can criticize and reject these four footprints of the Dhamma, they deserve rebuttal and criticism on four legitimate grounds in this very life.

<!-- @segment an4.30:7.2 -->

What four?

<!-- @segment an4.30:7.3 -->

If you reject the Dhamma footprint of contentment, then you must honor and praise those ascetics and brahmins who covet sensual pleasures with acute lust.

<!-- @segment an4.30:7.4 -->

If you reject the Dhamma footprint of good will, you must honor and praise those ascetics and brahmins who have ill will and malicious intent.

<!-- @segment an4.30:7.5 -->

If you reject the Dhamma footprint of right mindfulness, then you must honor and praise those ascetics and brahmins who are unmindful, with no situational awareness.

<!-- @segment an4.30:7.6 -->

If you reject the Dhamma footprint of right immersion, you must honor and praise those ascetics and brahmins who are scattered, with straying minds.

<!-- @segment an4.30:8.1 -->

If anyone imagines they can criticize and reject these four footprints of the Dhamma, they deserve rebuttal and criticism on four legitimate grounds in this very life.

<!-- @segment an4.30:8.2 -->

Even those who were loud and bold advocates of the doctrines of no-cause, inaction, and nihilism didn’t imagine that these four footprints of the Dhamma should be criticized or rejected.

<!-- @segment an4.30:8.3 -->

Why is that?

<!-- @segment an4.30:8.4 -->

For fear of being blamed, provoked, and faulted.

<!-- @segment an4.30:9.1 -->

One who has good will, ever mindful,

<!-- @segment an4.30:9.2 -->

serene within,

<!-- @segment an4.30:9.3 -->

training to remove desire,

<!-- @segment an4.30:9.4 -->

is called ‘a diligent one’.”
