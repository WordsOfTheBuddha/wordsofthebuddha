<!-- @segment an8.63:1.1 -->

Then a mendicant went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him,

<!-- @segment an8.63:1.2 -->

“Sir, may the Buddha please teach me Dhamma in brief. When I’ve heard it, I’ll live alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute.”

<!-- @segment an8.63:1.3 -->

“This is exactly how some futile people ask me for something.

<!-- @segment an8.63:1.4 -->

But when the teaching has been explained they think only of following me around.”

<!-- @segment an8.63:1.5 -->

“Sir, may the Buddha please teach me Dhamma in brief! May the Holy One teach me the Dhamma in brief! Hopefully I can understand the meaning of what the Buddha says! Hopefully I can be an heir of the Buddha’s teaching!”

<!-- @segment an8.63:1.6 -->

“Well then, mendicant, you should train like this:

<!-- @segment an8.63:1.7 -->

‘My mind will be steady and well settled internally. And bad, unskillful qualities that have arisen will not occupy my mind.’

<!-- @segment an8.63:1.8 -->

That’s how you should train.

<!-- @segment an8.63:2.1 -->

When your mind is steady and well settled internally, and bad, unskillful qualities that have arisen don’t occupy your mind, then you should train like this:

<!-- @segment an8.63:2.2 -->

‘I will develop the heart’s release by love. I’ll cultivate it, make it my vehicle and my basis, keep it up, consolidate it, and properly implement it.’

<!-- @segment an8.63:2.3 -->

That’s how you should train.

<!-- @segment an8.63:3.1 -->

When this immersion is developed and cultivated in this way, you should develop it while placing the mind and keeping it connected. You should develop it without placing the mind, merely keeping it connected. You should develop it without placing the mind or keeping it connected. You should develop it with rapture. You should develop it without rapture. You should develop it with pleasure. You should develop it with equanimity.

<!-- @segment an8.63:4.1 -->

When this immersion is well developed in this way, you should train like this:

<!-- @segment an8.63:4.2 -->

‘I will develop the heart’s release by compassion …’ …

<!-- @segment an8.63:4.3 -->

‘I will develop the heart’s release by rejoicing …’ …

<!-- @segment an8.63:4.4 -->

‘I will develop the heart’s release by equanimity. I’ll cultivate it, make it my vehicle and my basis, keep it up, consolidate it, and properly implement it.’

<!-- @segment an8.63:4.5 -->

That’s how you should train.

<!-- @segment an8.63:5.1 -->

When this immersion is well developed in this way, you should develop it while placing the mind and keeping it connected. You should develop it without placing the mind, merely keeping it connected. You should develop it without placing the mind or keeping it connected. You should develop it with rapture. You should develop it without rapture. You should develop it with pleasure. You should develop it with equanimity.

<!-- @segment an8.63:6.1 -->

When this immersion is well developed in this way, you should train like this:

<!-- @segment an8.63:6.2 -->

‘I’ll meditate observing an aspect of the body—keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world.’

<!-- @segment an8.63:6.3 -->

That’s how you should train.

<!-- @segment an8.63:7.1 -->

When this immersion is developed and cultivated in this way, you should develop it while placing the mind and keeping it connected. You should develop it without placing the mind, merely keeping it connected. You should develop it without placing the mind or keeping it connected. You should develop it with rapture. You should develop it without rapture. You should develop it with pleasure. You should develop it with equanimity.

<!-- @segment an8.63:8.1 -->

When this immersion is well developed in this way, you should train like this:

<!-- @segment an8.63:8.2 -->

‘I’ll meditate on an aspect of feelings …’ …

<!-- @segment an8.63:8.3 -->

‘I’ll meditate on an aspect of the mind …’ …

<!-- @segment an8.63:8.4 -->

‘I’ll meditate on an aspect of principles—keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world.’

<!-- @segment an8.63:8.5 -->

That’s how you should train.

<!-- @segment an8.63:9.1 -->

When this immersion is developed and cultivated in this way, you should develop it while placing the mind and keeping it connected. You should develop it without placing the mind, merely keeping it connected. You should develop it without placing the mind or keeping it connected. You should develop it with rapture. You should develop it without rapture. You should develop it with pleasure. You should develop it with equanimity.

<!-- @segment an8.63:10.1 -->

When this immersion is well developed in this way, wherever you walk, you’ll walk comfortably. Wherever you stand, you’ll stand comfortably. Wherever you sit, you’ll sit comfortably. Wherever you lie down, you’ll lie down comfortably.”

<!-- @segment an8.63:11.1 -->

When that mendicant had been given this advice by the Buddha, he rose from his seat, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right, before leaving.

<!-- @segment an8.63:11.2 -->

Then that mendicant, living alone, withdrawn, diligent, keen, and resolute, soon realized the supreme culmination of the spiritual path in this very life. He lived having achieved with his own insight the goal for which gentlemen rightly go forth from the lay life to homelessness.

<!-- @segment an8.63:11.3 -->

He understood: “Rebirth is ended; the spiritual journey has been completed; what had to be done has been done; there is nothing further for this place.”

<!-- @segment an8.63:11.4 -->

And that mendicant became one of the perfected.
