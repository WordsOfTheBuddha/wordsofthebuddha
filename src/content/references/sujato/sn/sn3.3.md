title: Old Age and Death
---

<!-- @segment sn3.3:1.1 -->

At Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment sn3.3:1.2 -->

Seated to one side, King Pasenadi said to the Buddha,

<!-- @segment sn3.3:1.3 -->

“Sir, for someone who has been reborn, is there any exemption from old age and death?”

<!-- @segment sn3.3:1.4 -->

“Great king, for someone who has been reborn, there’s no exemption from old age and death.

<!-- @segment sn3.3:1.5 -->

Even for well-to-do aristocrats,

<!-- @segment sn3.3:1.6 -->

brahmins,

<!-- @segment sn3.3:1.7 -->

or householders—rich, affluent, and wealthy, with lots of gold and silver, lots of property and assets, and lots of money and grain—when they’re born, there’s no exemption from old age and death.

<!-- @segment sn3.3:1.8 -->

Even for mendicants who are perfected—who have ended the defilements, completed the spiritual journey, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, achieved their heart’s goal, utterly ended the fetter of continued existence, and are rightly freed through enlightenment—their bodies are liable to break up and be laid to rest.”

<!-- @segment sn3.3:1.9 -->

That is what the Buddha said. …

<!-- @segment sn3.3:2.1 -->

“Fancy chariots of kings wear out,

<!-- @segment sn3.3:2.2 -->

and even this body gets old.

<!-- @segment sn3.3:2.3 -->

But the truth of the good never gets old—

<!-- @segment sn3.3:2.4 -->

so the good proclaim to the good.”
