<!-- @segment sn24.4:1.1 -->

At Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment sn24.4:1.2 -->

“Mendicants, when what exists, because of grasping what and insisting on what, does the view arise:

<!-- @segment sn24.4:1.3 -->

‘I might not be, and it might not be mine. I will not be, and it will not be mine’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.4:1.4 -->

“Our teachings are rooted in the Buddha. …”

<!-- @segment sn24.4:2.1 -->

“When form exists, because of grasping form and insisting on form, the view arises:

<!-- @segment sn24.4:2.2 -->

‘I might not be, and it might not be mine. I will not be, and it will not be mine.’

<!-- @segment sn24.4:2.3 -->

When feeling …

<!-- @segment sn24.4:2.4 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment sn24.4:2.5 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn24.4:2.6 -->

consciousness exists, because of grasping consciousness and insisting on consciousness, the view arises:

<!-- @segment sn24.4:2.7 -->

‘I might not be, and it might not be mine. I will not be, and it will not be mine.’

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.1 -->

What do you think, mendicants?

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.2 -->

Is form permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.3 -->

“Impermanent, sir.” …

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.4 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.5 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.6 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.7 -->

“Is feeling …

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.8 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.9 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.10 -->

consciousness permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.11 -->

“Impermanent, sir.” …

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.12 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.13 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.14 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.15 -->

“That which is seen, heard, thought, known, attained, sought, and explored by the mind: is that permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.16 -->

“Impermanent, sir.” …

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.17 -->

“But by not grasping what’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, would such a view arise?”

<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.18 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.4:3.19 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.4:4.1 -->

“When a noble disciple has given up doubt regarding these six grounds, and has given up doubt in suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the practice that leads to its cessation,

<!-- @segment sn24.4:4.2 -->

they’re called a noble disciple who is a stream-enterer, not liable to be reborn in the underworld, assured, destined for awakening.”
