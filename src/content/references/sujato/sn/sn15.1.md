title: Grass and Sticks

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery.

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.3 -->

There the Buddha addressed the mendicants,

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.4 -->

“Mendicants!”

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.5 -->

“Venerable sir,” they replied.

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.6 -->

The Buddha said this:

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.7 -->

“Mendicants, this transmigration has no known beginning.

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.8 -->

No first point is found of sentient beings roaming and transmigrating, shrouded by ignorance and fettered by craving.

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.9 -->

Suppose a person was to strip all the grass, sticks, branches, and leaves in the Black Plum Tree Land, gather them together into one pile, and chop them each into four inch pieces. They’d lay them down, saying:

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.10 -->

‘This is my mother, this is my grandmother.’ The grass, sticks, branches, and leaves of the Black Plum Tree Land would run out before that person’s mothers and grandmothers.

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.11 -->

Why is that?

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.12 -->

This transmigration has no known beginning.

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.13 -->

No first point is found of sentient beings roaming and transmigrating, shrouded by ignorance and fettered by craving.

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.14 -->

For such a long time you have undergone suffering, agony, and disaster, swelling the cemeteries.

<!-- @segment sn15.1:1.15 -->

This is quite enough for you to become disillusioned, dispassionate, and freed regarding all conditions.”
