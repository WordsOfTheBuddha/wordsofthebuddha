title: This is Mine
description: The Buddha teaches that when the five aggregates are grasped and insisted on, the wrong view arises that ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’, and that seeing their impermanence and suffering leads a noble disciple to become a stream-enterer.
qualities: suffering, attachment, doubt, ending, happiness, free from attachment
theme: cultivating discernment, wisdom
---

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.1 -->

At Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.2 -->

“Mendicants, when what exists, because of grasping what and insisting on what, does the view arise:

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.3 -->

‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.4 -->

“Our teachings are rooted in the Buddha. …”

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.5 -->

“When form exists, because of grasping form and insisting on form, the view arises:

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.6 -->

‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self.’

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.7 -->

When feeling …

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.8 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.9 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.10 -->

consciousness exists, because of grasping consciousness and insisting on consciousness, the view arises:

<!-- @segment sn24.2:1.11 -->

‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self.’

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.1 -->

What do you think, mendicants?

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.2 -->

Is form permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.3 -->

“Impermanent, sir.” …

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.4 -->

“Is feeling …

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.5 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.6 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.7 -->

consciousness permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.8 -->

“Impermanent, sir.” …

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.9 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.10 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.11 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.12 -->

“That which is seen, heard, thought, known, attained, sought, and explored by the mind: is that permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.13 -->

“Impermanent, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.14 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?”

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.15 -->

“Suffering, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.16 -->

“But by not grasping what’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, would the view arise:

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.17 -->

‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.2:2.18 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.2:3.1 -->

“When a noble disciple has given up doubt regarding these six grounds, and has given up doubt in suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the practice that leads to its cessation,

<!-- @segment sn24.2:3.2 -->

they’re called a noble disciple who is a stream-enterer, not liable to be reborn in the underworld, assured, destined for awakening.”
