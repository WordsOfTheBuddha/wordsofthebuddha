title: Winds
description: The Buddha teaches that when the five aggregates are grasped and insisted on, the wrong view arises that ‘Winds don’t blow; rivers don’t flow; pregnant women don’t give birth; the moon and stars neither rise nor set, but stand firm like a pillar’, and that seeing their impermanence and suffering leads a noble disciple to become a stream-enterer.

<!-- @segment sn24.1:1.1 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove.

<!-- @segment sn24.1:1.2 -->

The Buddha said this:

<!-- @segment sn24.1:1.3 -->

“Mendicants, when what exists, because of grasping what and insisting on what, does the view arise:

<!-- @segment sn24.1:1.4 -->

‘Winds don’t blow; rivers don’t flow; pregnant women don’t give birth; the moon and stars neither rise nor set, but stand firm like a pillar.’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:2.1 -->

“Our teachings are rooted in the Buddha. He is our guide and our refuge. Sir, may the Buddha himself please clarify the meaning of this. The mendicants will listen and remember it.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:2.2 -->

“Well then, mendicants, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:2.3 -->

“Yes, sir,” they replied.

<!-- @segment sn24.1:2.4 -->

The Buddha said this:

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.1 -->

“When form exists, because of grasping form and insisting on form, the view arises:

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.2 -->

‘Winds don’t blow; rivers don’t flow; pregnant women don’t give birth; the moon and stars neither rise nor set, but stand firm like a pillar.’

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.3 -->

When feeling …

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.4 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.5 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.6 -->

consciousness exists, because of grasping consciousness and insisting on consciousness, the view arises:

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.7 -->

‘Winds don’t blow; rivers don’t flow; pregnant women don’t give birth; the moon and stars neither rise nor set, but stand firm like a pillar.’

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.8 -->

What do you think, mendicants?

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.9 -->

Is form permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.10 -->

“Impermanent, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.11 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.12 -->

“Suffering, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.13 -->

“But by not grasping what’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, would the view arise:

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.14 -->

‘Winds don’t blow; rivers don’t flow; pregnant women don’t give birth; the moon and stars neither rise nor set, but stand firm like a pillar’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:3.15 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.1 -->

“Is feeling …

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.2 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.3 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.4 -->

consciousness permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.5 -->

“Impermanent, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.6 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.7 -->

“Suffering, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.8 -->

“But by not grasping what’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, would the view arise:

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.9 -->

‘Winds don’t blow; rivers don’t flow; pregnant women don’t give birth; the moon and stars neither rise nor set, but stand firm like a pillar’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.10 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.11 -->

“That which is seen, heard, thought, known, attained, sought, and explored by the mind: is that permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.12 -->

“Impermanent, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.13 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.14 -->

“Suffering, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.15 -->

“But by not grasping what’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, would the view arise:

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.16 -->

‘Winds don’t blow; rivers don’t flow; pregnant women don’t give birth; the moon and stars neither rise nor set, but stand firm like a pillar’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:4.17 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.1:5.1 -->

“When a noble disciple has given up doubt regarding these six grounds, and has given up doubt in suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the practice that leads to its cessation,

<!-- @segment sn24.1:5.2 -->

they’re called a noble disciple who is a stream-enterer, not liable to be reborn in the underworld, assured, destined for awakening.”
