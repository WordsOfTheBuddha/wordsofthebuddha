<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.1 -->

At Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.2 -->

“Mendicants, I will teach you the all.

<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.3 -->

Listen …

<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.4 -->

And what is the all?

<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.5 -->

It’s just the eye and sights, the ear and sounds, the nose and smells, the tongue and tastes, the body and touches, and the mind and ideas.

<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.6 -->

This is called the all.

<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.7 -->

Mendicants, suppose someone was to say:

<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.8 -->

‘I’ll deny this all and describe another all.’ They’d have no grounds for that claim,

<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.9 -->

they’d be stumped by questions, and, in addition, they’d get frustrated.

<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.10 -->

Why is that?

<!-- @segment sn35.23:1.11 -->

Because they’re out of their element.”
