title: This is My Self
description: The Buddha teaches that when the five aggregates are grasped and insisted on, the wrong view arises that ‘The self and the cosmos are one and the same. After death I will be that, permanent, everlasting, eternal, and imperishable’, and that seeing their impermanence and suffering leads a noble disciple to become a stream-enterer.
qualities: attachment, doubt, suffering, ending, free from attachment, wrong view
theme: cultivating discernment, wisdom
---

At Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment sn24.3:1.2 -->

“Mendicants, when what exists, because of grasping what and insisting on what, does the view arise:

<!-- @segment sn24.3:1.3 -->

‘The self and the cosmos are one and the same. After death I will be that, permanent, everlasting, eternal, and imperishable’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.3:1.4 -->

“Our teachings are rooted in the Buddha. …”

<!-- @segment sn24.3:2.1 -->

“When form exists, because of grasping form and insisting on form, the view arises:

<!-- @segment sn24.3:2.2 -->

‘The self and the cosmos are one and the same. After death I will be that, permanent, everlasting, eternal, and imperishable.’

<!-- @segment sn24.3:2.4 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn24.3:2.7 -->

‘The self and the cosmos are one and the same. After death I will be that, permanent, everlasting, eternal, and imperishable.’

What do you think, mendicants?

<!-- @segment sn24.3:3.2 -->

<!-- @segment sn24.3:3.18 -->

“Impermanent, sir.” …

<!-- @segment sn24.3:3.19 -->

<!-- @segment sn24.3:3.20 -->



<!-- @segment sn24.3:3.21 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.3:4.1 -->

“When a noble disciple has given up doubt regarding these six grounds, and has given up doubt in suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the practice that leads to its cessation,

<!-- @segment sn24.3:4.2 -->

they’re called a noble disciple who is a stream-enterer, not liable to be reborn in the underworld, assured, destined for awakening.”
