<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.1 -->

At Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.2 -->

“Mendicants, when what exists, because of grasping what and insisting on what,

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.3 -->

does someone regard things like this: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.4 -->

“Our teachings are rooted in the Buddha. …”

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.5 -->

“When form exists, because of grasping form and insisting on form …

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.6 -->

When consciousness exists, because of grasping consciousness and insisting on consciousness,

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.7 -->

someone regards it like this: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self.’

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.8 -->

What do you think, mendicants?

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.9 -->

Is form permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.10 -->

“Impermanent, sir.” …

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.11 -->

“But by not grasping what’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, would you regard it like this: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.12 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.13 -->

“Is feeling …

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.14 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.15 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.16 -->

consciousness permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.17 -->

“Impermanent, sir.” …

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.18 -->

“But by not grasping what’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, would you regard it like this: ‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.19 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.20 -->

“Seeing this …

<!-- @segment sn22.151:1.21 -->

They understand: ‘… there is nothing further for this place.’”
