title: The Self is Neither Happy Nor Suffering
description: The Buddha teaches that when the five aggregates are grasped and insisted on, the wrong view arises that ‘The self is neither happy nor suffering, and is healthy after death’, and that seeing their impermanence and suffering leads a noble disciple to become a stream-enterer.
qualities: suffering, attachment, wholesome, happiness, desire, right view
theme: cultivating discernment, wisdom

<!-- @segment sn24.96:1.1 -->

At Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment sn24.96:1.2 -->

“Mendicants, when what exists, because of grasping what and insisting on what, does the view arise:

<!-- @segment sn24.96:1.3 -->

‘The self is neither happy nor suffering, and is healthy after death’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:1.4 -->

“Our teachings are rooted in the Buddha. …”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:2.1 -->

“When form exists, because of grasping form and insisting on form, the view arises:

<!-- @segment sn24.96:2.2 -->

‘The self is neither happy nor suffering, and healthy after death.’

<!-- @segment sn24.96:2.3 -->

When feeling …

<!-- @segment sn24.96:2.4 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment sn24.96:2.5 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn24.96:2.6 -->

consciousness exists, because of grasping consciousness and insisting on consciousness, the view arises:

<!-- @segment sn24.96:2.7 -->

‘The self is neither happy nor suffering, and is healthy after death.’

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.1 -->

What do you think, mendicants?

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.2 -->

Is form permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.3 -->

“Impermanent, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.4 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.5 -->

“Suffering, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.6 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus:

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.7 -->

‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.8 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.9 -->

“Is feeling …

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.10 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.11 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.12 -->

consciousness permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.13 -->

“Impermanent, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.14 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.15 -->

“Suffering, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.16 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus:

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.17 -->

‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:3.18 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn24.96:4.1 -->

“So you should truly see any kind of form at all—past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: <em>all</em> form—with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

<!-- @segment sn24.96:4.2 -->

You should truly see any kind of feeling …

<!-- @segment sn24.96:4.3 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment sn24.96:4.4 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment sn24.96:4.5 -->

consciousness at all—past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: <em>all</em> consciousness—with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

<!-- @segment sn24.96:5.1 -->

Seeing this, a learned noble disciple grows disillusioned with form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness.

<!-- @segment sn24.96:5.2 -->

Being disillusioned, desire fades away. When desire fades away they’re freed. When they’re freed, they know they’re freed.

<!-- @segment sn24.96:5.3 -->

They understand: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’”
