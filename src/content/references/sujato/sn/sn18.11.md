<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.3 -->

Then Venerable Rāhula went up to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to him:

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.4 -->

“What do you think, Rāhula?

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.5 -->

Is the eye permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.6 -->

“Impermanent, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.7 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.8 -->

“Suffering, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.9 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus:

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.10 -->

‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.11 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.12 -->

“… the ear …

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.13 -->

the nose …

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.14 -->

the tongue …

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.15 -->

the body …

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.16 -->

Is the mind permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.17 -->

“Impermanent, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.18 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.19 -->

“Suffering, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.20 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus:

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.21 -->

‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.22 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.23 -->

“Seeing this, a learned noble disciple grows disillusioned with the eye, the ear, the nose, the tongue, the body, and the mind.

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.24 -->

Being disillusioned, desire fades away. When desire fades away they’re freed. When they’re freed, they know they’re freed.

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.25 -->

They understand: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’”

<!-- @segment sn18.11:1.26 -->

(The ten discourses of this series should be told in full the same way.)
