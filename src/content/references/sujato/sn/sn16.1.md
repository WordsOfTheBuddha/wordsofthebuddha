<!-- @segment sn16.1:1.1 -->

At Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment sn16.1:1.2 -->

“Mendicants, Kassapa is content with any kind of robe, and praises such contentment.

<!-- @segment sn16.1:1.3 -->

He doesn’t employ improper solicitation for the sake of a robe. He doesn’t worry if he doesn’t get a robe. And if he does get a robe, he uses it untied, uninfatuated, unattached, seeing the drawback, and understanding the escape.

<!-- @segment sn16.1:2.1 -->

Kassapa is content with any kind of almsfood …

<!-- @segment sn16.1:3.1 -->

Kassapa is content with any kind of lodging …

<!-- @segment sn16.1:4.1 -->

Kassapa is content with any kind of medicines and supplies for the sick …

<!-- @segment sn16.1:5.1 -->

So you should train like this: ‘We will be content with any kind of robe, and praise such contentment. We won’t employ improper solicitation for the sake of a robe.

<!-- @segment sn16.1:5.2 -->

We won’t worry if we don’t get a robe. And if we do get a robe, we’ll use it untied, uninfatuated, unattached, seeing the drawback, and understanding the escape.’

<!-- @segment sn16.1:5.3 -->

(All should be told in full the same way.)

<!-- @segment sn16.1:6.1 -->

‘We will be content with any kind of almsfood …’

<!-- @segment sn16.1:6.2 -->

‘We will be content with any kind of lodging …’

<!-- @segment sn16.1:6.3 -->

‘We will be content with any kind of medicines and supplies for the sick …’

<!-- @segment sn16.1:6.4 -->

That’s how you should train.

<!-- @segment sn16.1:6.5 -->

I will exhort you with the example of Kassapa or someone like him. You should practice accordingly.”
