<!-- @segment sn3.7:1.1 -->

At Sāvatthī.

<!-- @segment sn3.7:1.2 -->

Seated to one side, King Pasenadi said to the Buddha,

<!-- @segment sn3.7:1.3 -->

“Sir, when I’m sitting in court I see well-to-do aristocrats, brahmins, and householders—rich, affluent, and wealthy, with lots of gold and silver, lots of property and assets, and lots of money and grain. But they tell deliberate lies for the sake of sensual pleasures.

<!-- @segment sn3.7:1.4 -->

Then it occurred to me:

<!-- @segment sn3.7:1.5 -->

‘Enough with passing judgment today. Now my dearest will be known by the judgments he makes.’”

<!-- @segment sn3.7:2.1 -->

“That’s so true, great king! That’s so true!

<!-- @segment sn3.7:2.2 -->

Those who are well-to-do aristocrats, brahmins, and householders tell deliberate lies for the sake of sensual pleasures.

<!-- @segment sn3.7:2.3 -->

That is for their lasting harm and suffering.”

<!-- @segment sn3.7:2.4 -->

That is what the Buddha said. …

<!-- @segment sn3.7:3.1 -->

“Full of desire for possessions and pleasures,

<!-- @segment sn3.7:3.2 -->

greedy, infatuated by sensual pleasures;

<!-- @segment sn3.7:3.3 -->

they don’t notice that they’ve gone too far,

<!-- @segment sn3.7:3.4 -->

like fish swiftly snared.

<!-- @segment sn3.7:3.5 -->

It’ll be bitter later on;

<!-- @segment sn3.7:3.6 -->

for the result will be bad for them.”
