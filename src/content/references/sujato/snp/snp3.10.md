<!-- @segment snp3.10:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:1.3 -->

Then the mendicant Kokālika went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and said to him,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:1.4 -->

“Sir, Sāriputta and Moggallāna have corrupt wishes. They’ve fallen under the sway of corrupt wishes.”

<!-- @segment snp3.10:2.1 -->

When this was said, the Buddha said to Kokālika,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:2.2 -->

“Don’t say that, Kokālika! Don’t say that, Kokālika!

<!-- @segment snp3.10:2.3 -->

Have confidence in Sāriputta and Moggallāna,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:2.4 -->

they’re good monks.”

<!-- @segment snp3.10:3.1 -->

For a second time …

<!-- @segment snp3.10:3.2 -->

For a third time Kokālika said to the Buddha,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:3.3 -->

“Despite my faith and trust in the Buddha, Sāriputta and Moggallāna have corrupt wishes. They’ve fallen under the sway of corrupt wishes.”

<!-- @segment snp3.10:3.4 -->

For a third time, the Buddha said to Kokālika,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:3.5 -->

“Don’t say that, Kokālika! Don’t say that, Kokālika!

<!-- @segment snp3.10:3.6 -->

Have confidence in Sāriputta and Moggallāna,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:3.7 -->

they’re good monks.”

<!-- @segment snp3.10:4.1 -->

Then Kokālika rose from his seat, bowed, and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right, before leaving.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:4.2 -->

Not long after he left his body erupted with boils the size of mustard seeds.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:4.3 -->

The boils grew to the size of mung beans, then chickpeas, then jujube seeds, then jujubes, then myrobalans, then unripe wood-apples, then ripe wood-apples. Finally they burst open, and pus and blood oozed out.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:4.4 -->

Then the mendicant Kokālika died of that illness.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:4.5 -->

He was reborn in the Pink Lotus hell because of his resentment for Sāriputta and Moggallāna.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:5.1 -->

Then, late at night, the glorious divinity Sahampati, lighting up the entire Jeta’s Grove, went up to the Buddha, bowed, stood to one side, and said to him,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:5.2 -->

“Sir, the mendicant Kokālika has passed away.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:5.3 -->

He was reborn in the Pink Lotus hell because of his resentment for Sāriputta and Moggallāna.”

<!-- @segment snp3.10:5.4 -->

That’s what the divinity Sahampati said.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:5.5 -->

Then he bowed and respectfully circled the Buddha, keeping him on his right side, before vanishing right there.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:6.1 -->

Then, when the night had passed, the Buddha told the mendicants all that had happened.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:7.1 -->

When he said this, one of the mendicants said to the Buddha,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:7.2 -->

“Sir, how long is the lifespan in the Pink Lotus hell?”

<!-- @segment snp3.10:7.3 -->

“It’s long, mendicant.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:7.4 -->

It’s not easy to calculate how many years, how many hundreds or thousands or hundreds of thousands of years it lasts.”

<!-- @segment snp3.10:7.5 -->

“But sir, is it possible to give a simile?”

<!-- @segment snp3.10:7.6 -->

“It’s possible,” said the Buddha.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.1 -->

“Suppose there was a Kosalan load of twenty packs of sesame seed. And at the end of every hundred years someone would remove a single seed from it.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.2 -->

By this means the Kosalan load of twenty packs of sesame seed would run out faster than a single lifetime in the Abbuda hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.3 -->

Now, twenty lifetimes in the Abbuda hell equal one lifetime in the Nirabbuda hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.4 -->

Twenty lifetimes in the Nirabbuda hell equal one lifetime in the Ababa hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.5 -->

Twenty lifetimes in the Ababa hell equal one lifetime in the Aṭaṭa hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.6 -->

Twenty lifetimes in the Aṭaṭa hell equal one lifetime in the Ahaha hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.7 -->

Twenty lifetimes in the Ahaha hell equal one lifetime in the Yellow Lotus hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.8 -->

Twenty lifetimes in the Yellow Lotus hell equal one lifetime in the Fragrant Water Lily hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.9 -->

Twenty lifetimes in the Fragrant Water Lily hell equal one lifetime in the Blue Water Lily hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.10 -->

Twenty lifetimes in the Blue Water Lily hell equal one lifetime in the White Lotus hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.11 -->

Twenty lifetimes in the White Lotus hell equal one lifetime in the Pink Lotus hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.12 -->

The mendicant Kokālika has been reborn in the Pink Lotus hell because of his resentment for Sāriputta and Moggallāna.”

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.13 -->

That is what the Buddha said.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:8.14 -->

Then the Holy One, the Teacher, went on to say:

<!-- @segment snp3.10:9.1 -->

“A person is born

<!-- @segment snp3.10:9.2 -->

with an axe in their mouth.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:9.3 -->

A fool cuts themselves with it

<!-- @segment snp3.10:9.4 -->

when they say bad words.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:10.1 -->

When you praise someone worthy of criticism,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:10.2 -->

or criticize someone worthy of praise,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:10.3 -->

you choose a losing hand with your own mouth:

<!-- @segment snp3.10:10.4 -->

you’ll never find happiness that way.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:11.1 -->

A losing hand at dice is a trivial thing,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:11.2 -->

if all you lose is your money

<!-- @segment snp3.10:11.3 -->

and all you own, even yourself.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:11.4 -->

What’s a really terrible hand

<!-- @segment snp3.10:11.5 -->

is to hate the holy ones.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:12.1 -->

For a hundred thousand times a hundred million,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:12.2 -->

times five hundred and thirty-six times <j>a thousand times ten million years

<!-- @segment snp3.10:12.3 -->

a slanderer of noble ones goes to hell,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:12.4 -->

having aimed bad words and thoughts at them.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:13.1 -->

A liar goes to hell,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:13.2 -->

as does one who denies what they did.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:13.3 -->

Both are equal in the hereafter,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:13.4 -->

those men of base deeds.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:14.1 -->

Whoever wrongs a man who has done no wrong,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:14.2 -->

a pure man who has not a blemish,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:14.3 -->

the evil backfires on the fool,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:14.4 -->

like fine dust thrown upwind.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:15.1 -->

One addicted to the way of greed,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:15.2 -->

abuses others with their speech,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:15.3 -->

faithless, miserly, uncharitable,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:15.4 -->

stingy, addicted to backbiting.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:16.1 -->

Foul-mouthed, divisive, ignoble,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:16.2 -->

a baby-killer, wicked, wrongdoer,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:16.3 -->

worst of men, cursed, base-born—

<!-- @segment snp3.10:16.4 -->

quiet now, for you are bound for hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:17.1 -->

You stir up dust, causing harm,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:17.2 -->

when you, sinner, malign the good.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:17.3 -->

Having done many bad deeds,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:17.4 -->

you’ll go to the pit for a long time.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:18.1 -->

For no-one’s deeds are ever lost,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:18.2 -->

they return to their owner.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:18.3 -->

In the next life that stupid sinner

<!-- @segment snp3.10:18.4 -->

sees suffering in themselves.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:19.1 -->

They approach the place of impalement,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:19.2 -->

with its iron spikes, sharp blades, and iron stakes.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:19.3 -->

Then there is the food, which appropriately,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:19.4 -->

is like a red-hot iron ball.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:20.1 -->

For the speakers speak not sweetly,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:20.2 -->

they don’t hurry there, or find shelter.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:20.3 -->

They lie upon a spread of coals,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:20.4 -->

they enter a blazing mass of fire.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:21.1 -->

Wrapping them in a net,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:21.2 -->

they strike them there with iron hammers.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:21.3 -->

They come to blinding darkness,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:21.4 -->

which spreads about them like a fog.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:22.1 -->

Next they enter a copper pot,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:22.2 -->

a blazing mass of fire.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:22.3 -->

There they roast for a long time,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:22.4 -->

writhing in the masses of fire.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:23.1 -->

Then the sinner roasts there

<!-- @segment snp3.10:23.2 -->

in a mixture of pus and blood.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:23.3 -->

No matter where they settle,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:23.4 -->

everything they touch there hurts them.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:24.1 -->

The sinner roasts in

<!-- @segment snp3.10:24.2 -->

worm-infested water.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:24.3 -->

There’s not even a shore to go to,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:24.4 -->

for all around are the same kind of pots.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:25.1 -->

They enter the Sword-Leaf Forest,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:25.2 -->

so sharp they cut their body to pieces.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:25.3 -->

Having grabbed the tongue with a hook,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:25.4 -->

they stab it, slashing back and forth.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:26.1 -->

Then they approach the impassable Vetaraṇī River,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:26.2 -->

with its sharp blades, its razor blades.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:26.3 -->

Idiots fall into it,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:26.4 -->

the wicked who have done wicked deeds.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:27.1 -->

There dogs all brown and spotted,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:27.2 -->

and raven flocks, and greedy jackals

<!-- @segment snp3.10:27.3 -->

devour them as they wail,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:27.4 -->

while hawks and crows attack them.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:28.1 -->

Hard, alas, is the life here

<!-- @segment snp3.10:28.2 -->

that sinners endure.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:28.3 -->

That’s why for the rest of this life

<!-- @segment snp3.10:28.4 -->

a person ought do their duty without fail.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:29.1 -->

Experts have counted the loads of sesame

<!-- @segment snp3.10:29.2 -->

as compared to the Pink Lotus Hell.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:29.3 -->

For there are five times ten thousand times ten million,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:29.4 -->

plus another twelve hundred times ten million years—

<!-- @segment snp3.10:30.1 -->

as painful as life is said to be in hell,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:30.2 -->

that’s how long one must dwell there.

<!-- @segment snp3.10:30.3 -->

That’s why, for those who are pure, well-behaved, <j>full of good qualities,

<!-- @segment snp3.10:30.4 -->

one should always guard one’s speech and mind.”
