1. On the Brahmin Emissaries

<!-- @segment dn6:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment dn6:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Vesālī, at the Great Wood, in the hall with the peaked roof.

<!-- @segment dn6:1.3 -->

Now at that time several brahmin emissaries from Kosala and Magadha were residing in Vesālī on some business.

<!-- @segment dn6:1.4 -->

They heard:

<!-- @segment dn6:1.5 -->

“It seems the ascetic Gotama—a Sakyan, gone forth from a Sakyan family—is staying near Vesālī, at the Great Wood, in the hall with the peaked roof.

<!-- @segment dn6:1.6 -->

He has this good reputation:

<!-- @segment dn6:1.7 -->

‘That Blessed One is perfected, a fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.’

<!-- @segment dn6:1.8 -->

He has realized with his own insight this world—with its gods, Māras, and divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, gods and humans—and he makes it known to others.

<!-- @segment dn6:1.9 -->

He proclaims a teaching that’s good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, meaningful and well-phrased. And he reveals a spiritual practice that’s entirely full and pure.

<!-- @segment dn6:1.10 -->

It’s good to see such perfected ones.”

<!-- @segment dn6:2.1 -->

Then they went to the hall with the peaked roof in the Great Wood to see the Buddha.

<!-- @segment dn6:2.2 -->

Now, at that time Venerable Nāgita was the Buddha’s attendant.

<!-- @segment dn6:2.3 -->

The brahmin emissaries went up to him and said,

<!-- @segment dn6:2.4 -->

“Worthy Nāgita, where is the worthy Gotama at present?

<!-- @segment dn6:2.5 -->

For we want to see him.”

<!-- @segment dn6:2.6 -->

“It’s the wrong time to see the Buddha; he is on retreat.”

<!-- @segment dn6:2.7 -->

So right there the brahmin emissaries sat down to one side, thinking,

<!-- @segment dn6:2.8 -->

“We’ll go only after we’ve seen the worthy Gotama.”

<!-- @segment dn6:3.0 -->

2. On Oṭṭhaddha the Licchavi

<!-- @segment dn6:3.1 -->

Oṭṭhaddha the Licchavi together with a large assembly of Licchavis also approached Nāgita at the hall with the peaked roof. He bowed, stood to one side, and said to Nāgita,

<!-- @segment dn6:3.2 -->

“Worthy Nāgita, where is the Blessed One at present, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha?

<!-- @segment dn6:3.3 -->

For we want to see him.”

<!-- @segment dn6:3.4 -->

“It’s the wrong time to see the Buddha; he is on retreat.”

<!-- @segment dn6:3.5 -->

So right there Oṭṭhaddha also sat down to one side, thinking,

<!-- @segment dn6:3.6 -->

“I’ll go only after I’ve seen the Blessed One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha.”

<!-- @segment dn6:4.1 -->

Then the novice Sīha approached Nāgita. He bowed, stood to one side, and said to Nāgita,

<!-- @segment dn6:4.2 -->

“Honorable Kassapa, these several brahmin emissaries from Kosala and Magadha, and also Oṭṭhaddha the Licchavi together with a large assembly of Licchavis, have come here to see the Buddha. It’d be good if these people got to see the Buddha.”

<!-- @segment dn6:4.3 -->

“Well then, Sīha, tell the Buddha yourself.”

<!-- @segment dn6:4.4 -->

“Yes, sir,” replied Sīha. He went to the Buddha, bowed, stood to one side, and told him of the people waiting to see him, adding:

<!-- @segment dn6:4.5 -->



<!-- @segment dn6:4.6 -->

“Sir, it’d be good if these people got to see the Buddha.”

<!-- @segment dn6:4.7 -->

“Well then, Sīha, spread out a seat in the shade of the dwelling.”

<!-- @segment dn6:4.8 -->

“Yes, sir,” replied Sīha, and he did so.

<!-- @segment dn6:4.9 -->

Then the Buddha came out of his dwelling and sat in the shade of the dwelling on the seat spread out.

<!-- @segment dn6:5.1 -->

Then the brahmin emissaries went up to the Buddha, and exchanged greetings with him.

<!-- @segment dn6:5.2 -->

When the greetings and polite conversation were over, they sat down to one side.

<!-- @segment dn6:5.3 -->

Oṭṭhaddha the Licchavi together with a large assembly of Licchavis also went up to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. Oṭṭhaddha said to the Buddha, “Sir, a few days ago Sunakkhatta the Licchavi came to me and said:

<!-- @segment dn6:5.4 -->

‘Mahāli, soon I will have been living in dependence on the Buddha for three years. I see heavenly sights that are pleasant, sensual, and arousing, but I don’t hear heavenly sounds that are pleasant, sensual, and arousing.’

<!-- @segment dn6:5.5 -->

The heavenly sounds that Sunakkhatta cannot hear: do such sounds really exist or not?”

<!-- @segment dn6:5.6.0 -->

2.1. One-Sided Immersion

<!-- @segment dn6:5.7 -->

“Such sounds really do exist, but Sunakkhatta cannot hear them.”

<!-- @segment dn6:6.1 -->

“What is the cause, sir, what is the reason why Sunakkhatta cannot hear them, even though they really do exist?”

<!-- @segment dn6:6.2 -->

“Mahāli, take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the eastern quarter in one aspect: so as to see heavenly sights but not to hear heavenly sounds.

<!-- @segment dn6:6.3 -->

When they have developed immersion for that purpose,

<!-- @segment dn6:6.4 -->

they see heavenly sights but don’t hear heavenly sounds.

<!-- @segment dn6:6.5 -->

Why is that?

<!-- @segment dn6:6.6 -->

Because that is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way.

<!-- @segment dn6:7.1 -->

Furthermore, take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the southern quarter in one aspect …

<!-- @segment dn6:7.2 -->

western quarter …

<!-- @segment dn6:7.3 -->

northern quarter …

<!-- @segment dn6:7.4 -->

above, below, across …

<!-- @segment dn6:7.5 -->



<!-- @segment dn6:7.6 -->



<!-- @segment dn6:7.7 -->



<!-- @segment dn6:7.8 -->

That is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way.

<!-- @segment dn6:8.1 -->

Take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the eastern quarter in one aspect: so as to hear heavenly sounds but not to see heavenly sights.

<!-- @segment dn6:8.2 -->

<!-- @segment dn6:8.3 -->

they hear heavenly sounds but don’t see heavenly sights.

<!-- @segment dn6:8.4 -->

Why is that?

<!-- @segment dn6:8.5 -->

Because that is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way.

Furthermore, take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the southern quarter in one aspect …

western quarter …

Take a mendicant who has developed immersion to the eastern quarter in both aspects: so as to hear heavenly sounds and see heavenly sights.

<!-- @segment dn6:10.2 -->

When they have developed immersion for that purpose,

<!-- @segment dn6:10.3 -->

they see heavenly sights and hear heavenly sounds.

<!-- @segment dn6:10.4 -->

<!-- @segment dn6:10.5 -->

Because that is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way.

<!-- @segment dn6:11.2 -->

western quarter …

<!-- @segment dn6:11.3 -->

northern quarter …

<!-- @segment dn6:11.4 -->

above, below, across …

<!-- @segment dn6:11.5 -->

<!-- @segment dn6:11.8 -->

That is how it is for a mendicant who develops immersion in that way.

<!-- @segment dn6:11.9 -->

This is the cause, Mahāli, this is the reason why Sunakkhatta cannot hear heavenly sounds that are pleasant, sensual, and arousing, even though they really do exist.”

<!-- @segment dn6:12.1 -->

“Surely the mendicants must lead the spiritual life under the Buddha for the sake of realizing such a development of immersion?”

<!-- @segment dn6:12.2 -->

“No, Mahāli, the mendicants don’t lead the spiritual life under me for the sake of realizing such a development of immersion.

<!-- @segment dn6:12.3 -->

There are other things that are finer, for the sake of which the mendicants lead the spiritual life under me.”

<!-- @segment dn6:13.0 -->

2.2. The Four Noble Fruits

<!-- @segment dn6:13.1 -->

“But sir, what are those finer things?”

<!-- @segment dn6:13.2 -->

“Firstly, Mahāli, with the ending of three fetters a mendicant is a stream-enterer, not liable to be reborn in the underworld, assured, destined for awakening.

<!-- @segment dn6:13.3 -->

This is one of the finer things for the sake of which the mendicants lead the spiritual life under me.

<!-- @segment dn6:13.4 -->

Furthermore, a mendicant—with the ending of three fetters, and the weakening of greed, hate, and delusion—is a once-returner. They come back to this world once only, then make an end of suffering.

<!-- @segment dn6:13.5 -->

This too is one of the finer things.

<!-- @segment dn6:13.6 -->

Furthermore, with the ending of the five lower fetters, a mendicant is reborn spontaneously and will become extinguished there, not liable to return from that world.

<!-- @segment dn6:13.7 -->

This too is one of the finer things.

<!-- @segment dn6:13.8 -->

Furthermore, a mendicant has realized the undefiled freedom of heart and freedom by wisdom in this very life, and lives having realized it with their own insight due to the ending of defilements.

<!-- @segment dn6:13.9 -->

This too is one of the finer things.

<!-- @segment dn6:13.10 -->

These are the finer things, for the sake of which the mendicants lead the spiritual life under me.”

<!-- @segment dn6:14.0 -->

2.3. The Noble Eightfold Path

<!-- @segment dn6:14.1 -->

“But, sir, is there a path and a practice for realizing these things?”

<!-- @segment dn6:14.2 -->

“There is, Mahāli.”

<!-- @segment dn6:14.3 -->

“Well, what is it?”

<!-- @segment dn6:14.4 -->

“It is simply this noble eightfold path, that is:

<!-- @segment dn6:14.5 -->

right view, right purpose, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right immersion.

<!-- @segment dn6:14.6 -->

This is the path and the practice for realizing these things.

2.4. On the Two Renunciates

<!-- @segment dn6:15.1 -->

This one time, Mahāli, I was staying near Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery.

<!-- @segment dn6:15.2 -->

Then two renunciates—

<!-- @segment dn6:15.3 -->

the wanderer Muṇḍiya and Jāliya, the pupil of the wood-bowl ascetic—came and exchanged greetings with me.

<!-- @segment dn6:15.4 -->

When the greetings and polite conversation were over, they stood to one side and said to me:

<!-- @segment dn6:15.5 -->

‘Reverend Gotama, are the soul and the body one and the same, or is the soul one thing, the body another?’

<!-- @segment dn6:16.1 -->

‘Well then, reverends, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.’

<!-- @segment dn6:16.2 -->

‘Yes, reverend,’ they replied.

<!-- @segment dn6:16.3 -->

I said this:

<!-- @segment dn6:16.4 -->

‘Take the case when a Realized One arises in the world, perfected, a fully awakened Buddha …

<!-- @segment dn6:16.5 -->

That’s how a mendicant is accomplished in ethics. …

<!-- @segment dn6:16.6 -->



<!-- @segment dn6:16.7 -->

They enter and remain in the first absorption.

<!-- @segment dn6:16.8 -->

When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them:

<!-- @segment dn6:16.9 -->

“The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”?’

<!-- @segment dn6:16.10 -->

‘It would, reverend.’

<!-- @segment dn6:16.11 -->



<!-- @segment dn6:16.12 -->

‘But reverends, I know and see like this.

<!-- @segment dn6:16.13 -->

Nevertheless, I do not say:

<!-- @segment dn6:16.14 -->

“The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul and the body are different things”. …

<!-- @segment dn6:17.1 -->

They enter and remain in the second absorption …

<!-- @segment dn6:17.2 -->

third absorption …

<!-- @segment dn6:17.3 -->

fourth absorption.

<!-- @segment dn6:17.4 -->

When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them:

<!-- @segment dn6:17.5 -->

“The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”?’

<!-- @segment dn6:17.6 -->

‘It would, reverend.’

<!-- @segment dn6:17.7 -->



<!-- @segment dn6:17.8 -->

‘But reverends, I know and see like this.

<!-- @segment dn6:17.9 -->

Nevertheless, I do not say:

<!-- @segment dn6:17.10 -->

“The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”. …

<!-- @segment dn6:18.1 -->

They project and extend the mind toward knowledge and vision …

<!-- @segment dn6:18.2 -->

When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them:

<!-- @segment dn6:18.3 -->

“The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”?’

<!-- @segment dn6:18.4 -->

Nevertheless, I do not say:

<!-- @segment dn6:18.9 -->

“The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”. …

<!-- @segment dn6:19.1 -->

They understand: “… there is nothing further for this place.”

<!-- @segment dn6:19.2 -->

When a mendicant knows and sees like this, would it be appropriate to say of them:

<!-- @segment dn6:19.3 -->

“The soul and the body are one and the same” or “The soul is one thing, the body another”?’

<!-- @segment dn6:19.4 -->
