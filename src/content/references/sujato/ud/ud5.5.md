<!-- @segment ud5.5:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:1.3 -->

Now, at that time it was the sabbath, and the Buddha was sitting surrounded by the Saṅgha of monks.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:2.1 -->

And then, as the night was getting late, in the first watch of the night, Venerable Ānanda rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha and said,

<!-- @segment ud5.5:2.2 -->

“Sir, the night is getting late. It is the first watch of the night, and the Saṅgha of monks has been sitting long.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:2.3 -->

Please, sir, may the Buddha recite the monastic code to the monks.”

<!-- @segment ud5.5:2.4 -->

But when he said this, the Buddha kept silent.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:3.1 -->

For a second time, as the night was getting late, in the middle watch of the night, Ānanda rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha and said,

<!-- @segment ud5.5:3.2 -->

“Sir, the night is getting late. It is the middle watch of the night, and the Saṅgha of monks has been sitting long.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:3.3 -->

Please, sir, may the Buddha recite the monastic code to the monks.”

<!-- @segment ud5.5:3.4 -->

But for a second time the Buddha kept silent.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:4.1 -->

For a third time, as the night was getting late, in the last watch of the night, as dawn stirred, bringing joy to the night, Ānanda rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his cupped palms toward the Buddha and said,

<!-- @segment ud5.5:4.2 -->

“Sir, the night is getting late. It is the last watch of the night and dawn stirs, bringing joy to the night.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:4.3 -->

Please, sir, may the Buddha recite the monastic code to the monks.”

<!-- @segment ud5.5:4.4 -->

“Ānanda, the assembly is not pure.”

<!-- @segment ud5.5:5.1 -->

Then Venerable Mahāmoggallāna thought,

<!-- @segment ud5.5:5.2 -->

“Who is the Buddha talking about?”

<!-- @segment ud5.5:5.4 -->

Then he focused on encompassing the minds of everyone in the Saṅgha.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:5.5 -->

He saw that unethical individual, of bad qualities, filthy, with suspicious behavior, underhand, no true ascetic or spiritual practitioner—though claiming to be one—rotten inside, festering, and depraved, sitting in the middle of the Saṅgha.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:5.6 -->

When he saw him he rose from his seat, went up to him and said,

<!-- @segment ud5.5:5.7 -->

“Get up, reverend. The Buddha has seen you.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:5.8 -->

You can’t live in communion with the monks.”

<!-- @segment ud5.5:5.9 -->

But when he said this, that individual kept silent.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:6.1 -->

For a second time and a third time,

<!-- @segment ud5.5:6.2 -->

he asked that monk to leave.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:6.5 -->

But for a third time that individual kept silent.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:7.1 -->

Then Venerable Mahāmoggallāna took that individual by the arm, ejected him out the gate, and bolted the door. Then he went up to the Buddha, and said to him,

<!-- @segment ud5.5:7.2 -->

“I have ejected that individual.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:7.3 -->

The assembly is pure.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:7.4 -->

Please, sir, may the Buddha recite the monastic code to the monks.”

<!-- @segment ud5.5:7.5 -->

“It’s incredible, Moggallāna, it’s amazing,

<!-- @segment ud5.5:7.6 -->

how that futile man waited to be taken by the arm!”

<!-- @segment ud5.5:8.1 -->

Then the Buddha said to the monks,

<!-- @segment ud5.5:8.2 -->

“From this day forth, monks, I will not perform the sabbath or recite the monastic code.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:8.3 -->

Now you should perform the sabbath and recite the monastic code.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:8.4 -->

It’s impossible, monks, it can’t happen that a Realized One could recite the monastic code in an impure assembly.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:9.1 -->

Seeing these eight incredible and amazing things the titans love the ocean.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:9.2 -->

What eight?

<!-- @segment ud5.5:10.1 -->

The ocean gradually slants, slopes, and inclines, with no abrupt precipice.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:10.3 -->

This is the first thing the titans love about the ocean.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:11.1 -->

Furthermore, the ocean is consistent and doesn’t overflow its boundaries.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:11.3 -->

This is the second thing the titans love about the ocean.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:12.1 -->

Furthermore, the ocean doesn’t accommodate a carcass, but quickly carries it to the shore and strands it on the beach.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:12.3 -->

This is the third thing the titans love about the ocean.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:13.1 -->

Furthermore, when they reach the ocean, all the great rivers—that is, the Ganges, Yamunā, Aciravatī, Sarabhū, and Mahī—lose their names and clans and are simply reckoned as ‘the ocean’.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:13.3 -->

This is the fourth thing the titans love about the ocean.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:14.1 -->

Furthermore, for all the world’s streams that reach it, and the showers that fall from the sky, the ocean never empties or fills up.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:14.3 -->

This is the fifth thing the titans love about the ocean.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:15.1 -->

Furthermore, the ocean has just one taste, the taste of salt.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:15.3 -->

This is the sixth thing the titans love about the ocean.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:16.1 -->

Furthermore, the ocean is full of many kinds of treasures, such as pearls, gems, beryl, conch, quartz, coral, silver, native gold, rubies, and emeralds.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:16.3 -->

This is the seventh thing the titans love about the ocean.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:17.1 -->

Furthermore, many great beings live in the ocean, such as leviathans, leviathan-gulpers, leviathan-gulper-gulpers, titans, dragons, and centaurs. In the ocean there are life-forms a hundred leagues long, or even two hundred, three hundred, four hundred, or five hundred leagues long.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:17.3 -->

This is the eighth thing the titans love about the ocean.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:17.4 -->

Seeing these eight incredible and amazing things the titans love the ocean.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:18.1 -->

In the same way, seeing eight incredible and amazing things, mendicants, the mendicants love this teaching and training.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:18.2 -->

What eight?

<!-- @segment ud5.5:19.1 -->

The ocean gradually slants, slopes, and inclines, with no abrupt precipice.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:19.2 -->

In the same way in this teaching and training the penetration to enlightenment comes from gradual training, progress, and practice, not abruptly.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:19.4 -->

This is the first thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:20.1 -->

The ocean is consistent and doesn’t overflow its boundaries.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:20.2 -->

In the same way, when a training rule is laid down for my disciples they wouldn’t break it even for the sake of their own life.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:20.4 -->

This is the second thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:21.1 -->

The ocean doesn’t accommodate a carcass, but quickly carries it to the shore and strands it on the beach.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:21.2 -->

In the same way, the Saṅgha doesn’t accommodate an individual who is unethical, of bad qualities, filthy, with suspicious behavior, underhand, no true ascetic or spiritual practitioner—though claiming to be one—rotten inside, festering, and depraved. But they quickly gather and expel them. Even if such an individual is sitting in the middle of the Saṅgha, they’re far from the Saṅgha, and the Saṅgha is far from them.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:21.4 -->

This is the third thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:22.1 -->

Furthermore, when they reach the ocean, all the great rivers—that is, the Ganges, Yamunā, Aciravatī, Sarabhū, and Mahī—lose their names and clans and are simply reckoned as ‘the ocean’.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:22.2 -->

In the same way, when they go forth from the lay life to homelessness, all four classes—aristocrats, brahmins, peasants, and menials—lose their former names and clans and are simply reckoned as ‘ascetics who follow the Sakyan’.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:22.4 -->

This is the fourth thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:23.1 -->

For all the world’s streams that reach it, and the showers that fall from the sky, the ocean never empties or fills up.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:23.2 -->

In the same way, though several mendicants become fully extinguished in the element of extinguishment with no residue, the element of extinguishment never empties or fills up.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:23.4 -->

This is the fifth thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:24.1 -->

The ocean has just one taste, the taste of salt.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:24.2 -->

In the same way, this teaching and training has one taste, the taste of freedom.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:24.4 -->

This is the sixth thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:25.1 -->

The ocean is full of many kinds of treasures, such as pearls, gems, beryl, conch, quartz, coral, silver, native gold, rubies, and emeralds.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:25.2 -->

In the same way, this teaching and training is full of many kinds of treasures, such as the four kinds of mindfulness meditation, the four right efforts, the four bases of psychic power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven awakening factors, and the noble eightfold path.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:25.4 -->

This is the seventh thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:26.1 -->

Many great beings live in the ocean, such as leviathans, leviathan-gulpers, leviathan-gulper-gulpers, titans, dragons, and centaurs. In the ocean there are life-forms a hundred leagues long, or even two hundred, three hundred, four hundred, or five hundred leagues long.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:26.2 -->

In the same way, great beings live in this teaching and training, and these are those beings. The stream-enterer and the one practicing to realize the fruit of stream-entry. The once-returner and the one practicing to realize the fruit of once-return. The non-returner and the one practicing to realize the fruit of non-return. The perfected one, and the one practicing for perfection.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:26.4 -->

This is the eighth thing the mendicants love about this teaching and training.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:26.5 -->

Seeing these eight incredible and amazing things, the mendicants love this teaching and training.”

<!-- @segment ud5.5:27.1 -->

Then, understanding this matter, on that occasion the Buddha expressed this heartfelt sentiment:

<!-- @segment ud5.5:28.1 -->

“The rain saturates things that are covered up;

<!-- @segment ud5.5:28.2 -->

it doesn’t saturate things that are open.

<!-- @segment ud5.5:28.3 -->

Therefore you should open up a covered thing,

<!-- @segment ud5.5:28.4 -->

so the rain will not saturate it.”
