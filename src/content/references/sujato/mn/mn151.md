<!-- @segment mn151:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment mn151:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground.

<!-- @segment mn151:1.3 -->

Then in the late afternoon, Sāriputta came out of retreat and went to the Buddha. He bowed and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to him,

<!-- @segment mn151:2.1 -->

“Sāriputta, your faculties are so very clear, and your complexion is pure and bright.

<!-- @segment mn151:2.2 -->

What kind of meditation are you usually practicing these days?”

<!-- @segment mn151:2.3 -->

“Sir, these days I usually practice the meditation on emptiness.”

<!-- @segment mn151:2.4 -->

“Good, good, Sāriputta!

<!-- @segment mn151:2.5 -->

It seems you usually practice the meditation of a great man.

<!-- @segment mn151:2.6 -->

For emptiness is the meditation of a great man.

<!-- @segment mn151:2.7 -->



<!-- @segment mn151:3.1 -->

Now, a mendicant might wish:

<!-- @segment mn151:3.2 -->

‘May I usually practice the meditation on emptiness.’ So they should reflect:

<!-- @segment mn151:3.3 -->

‘Along the path that I went for alms, or in the place I wandered for alms, or along the path that I returned from alms, was there any desire or greed or hate or delusion or aversion in my heart for sights known by the eye?’

<!-- @segment mn151:3.4 -->

Suppose that, upon checking, a mendicant knows that

<!-- @segment mn151:3.5 -->

there was such desire or greed or hate or delusion or aversion in their heart, they should make an effort to give up those unskillful qualities.

<!-- @segment mn151:3.6 -->

But suppose that, upon checking, a mendicant knows that

<!-- @segment mn151:3.7 -->

there was no such desire or greed or hate or delusion or aversion in their heart, they should meditate with rapture and joy, training day and night in skillful qualities.
