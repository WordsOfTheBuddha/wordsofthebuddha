<!-- @segment mn126:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment mn126:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, the squirrels’ feeding ground.

<!-- @segment mn126:2.1 -->

Then Venerable Bhūmija robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, went to the home of Prince Jayasena, where he sat on the seat spread out.

<!-- @segment mn126:3.1 -->

Then Jayasena approached and exchanged greetings with him.

<!-- @segment mn126:3.2 -->

When the greetings and polite conversation were over, he sat down to one side and said to Bhūmija:

<!-- @segment mn126:3.3 -->

“Worthy Bhūmija, there are some ascetics and brahmins who have this doctrine and view:

<!-- @segment mn126:3.4 -->

‘If you make a wish and lead the spiritual life, you can’t win the fruit.

<!-- @segment mn126:3.5 -->

If you don’t make a wish and lead the spiritual life, you can’t win the fruit.

<!-- @segment mn126:3.6 -->

If you both make a wish and don’t make a wish and lead the spiritual life, you can’t win the fruit.

<!-- @segment mn126:3.7 -->

If you neither make a wish nor don’t make a wish and lead the spiritual life, you can’t win the fruit.’

<!-- @segment mn126:3.8 -->

What does the worthy Bhūmija’s Teacher say about this? How does he explain it?”

<!-- @segment mn126:4.1 -->

“Prince, I haven’t heard and learned this in the presence of the Buddha.

<!-- @segment mn126:4.2 -->

But it’s possible that he might explain it like this:

<!-- @segment mn126:4.3 -->

‘If you lead the spiritual life irrationally, you can’t win the fruit, regardless of whether you make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:4.4 -->

you don’t make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:4.5 -->

you both do and do not make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:4.6 -->

or you neither do nor don’t make a wish.

<!-- @segment mn126:4.7 -->

But if you lead the spiritual life rationally, you can win the fruit, regardless of whether you make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:4.8 -->

you don’t make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:4.9 -->

you both do and do not make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:4.10 -->

or you neither do nor don’t make a wish.’

<!-- @segment mn126:4.11 -->

I haven’t heard and learned this in the presence of the Buddha.

<!-- @segment mn126:4.12 -->

But it’s possible that he might explain it like that.”

<!-- @segment mn126:5.1 -->

“If that’s what your teacher says, worthy Bhūmija, he clearly stands head and shoulders above all the various other ascetics and brahmins.”

<!-- @segment mn126:9.1 -->

There are some ascetics and brahmins who have wrong view, wrong purpose, wrong speech, wrong action, wrong livelihood, wrong effort, wrong mindfulness, and wrong immersion. If they lead the spiritual life, they can’t win the fruit, regardless of whether they make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:9.2 -->

they don’t make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:9.3 -->

they both do and do not make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:9.4 -->

or they neither do nor don’t make a wish.

<!-- @segment mn126:9.5 -->

Why is that?

<!-- @segment mn126:9.6 -->

Because that’s an irrational way to win the fruit.

<!-- @segment mn126:10.1 -->

Suppose there was a person in need of oil. While wandering in search of oil, they tried heaping sand in a bucket, sprinkling it little by little with water, and pressing it out.

<!-- @segment mn126:10.2 -->

But by doing this, they couldn’t extract any oil, regardless of whether they made a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:10.3 -->

didn’t make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:10.4 -->

both did and did not make a wish,

<!-- @segment mn126:10.5 -->

or neither did nor did not make a wish.

<!-- @segment mn126:10.6 -->

Why is that?

<!-- @segment mn126:10.7 -->

Because that’s an irrational way to extract oil.

<!-- @segment mn126:10.8 -->

And so it is for any ascetics and brahmins who have wrong view, wrong purpose, wrong speech, wrong action, wrong livelihood, wrong effort, wrong mindfulness, and wrong immersion. If they lead the spiritual life, they can’t win the fruit, regardless of whether or not they make a wish.
