<!-- @segment mn48:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment mn48:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Kosambī, in Ghosita’s Monastery.

<!-- @segment mn48:2.1 -->

Now at that time the mendicants of Kosambī were fighting, quarreling, and disputing, continually wounding each other with barbed words.

<!-- @segment mn48:2.2 -->

They couldn’t persuade each other or be persuaded, nor could they convince each other or be convinced.

<!-- @segment mn48:3.1 -->

Then a mendicant went up to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him what was happening.

<!-- @segment mn48:3.2 -->



<!-- @segment mn48:4.1 -->

So the Buddha addressed one of the monks,

<!-- @segment mn48:4.2 -->

“Please, monk, in my name tell those mendicants that

<!-- @segment mn48:4.3 -->

the teacher summons them.”

<!-- @segment mn48:4.4 -->

“Yes, sir,” that monk replied. He went to those monks and said,

<!-- @segment mn48:4.5 -->

“Venerables, the teacher summons you.”

<!-- @segment mn48:4.6 -->

“Yes, reverend,” those monks replied. They went to the Buddha, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Buddha said to them,

<!-- @segment mn48:4.7 -->

“Is it really true, mendicants, that you have been fighting, quarreling, and disputing, continually wounding each other with barbed words?

<!-- @segment mn48:4.8 -->

And that you can’t persuade each other or be persuaded, nor can you convince each other or be convinced?”

<!-- @segment mn48:4.9 -->

“Yes, sir,” they said.

<!-- @segment mn48:5.1 -->

“What do you think, mendicants?

<!-- @segment mn48:5.2 -->

When you’re fighting, quarreling, and disputing, continually wounding each other with barbed words, are you treating your spiritual companions with kindness by way of body, speech, and mind, both in public and in private?”

<!-- @segment mn48:5.3 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment mn48:5.4 -->

“So it seems that when you’re arguing you are not treating each other with kindness.

<!-- @segment mn48:5.5 -->

So what then do you know and see, you futile men, that you behave in such a way?

<!-- @segment mn48:5.6 -->

This will be for your lasting harm and suffering.”

<!-- @segment mn48:6.1 -->

Then the Buddha said to the mendicants:

<!-- @segment mn48:6.2 -->

“Mendicants, these six warm-hearted qualities make for fondness and respect, conducing to inclusion, harmony, and unity, without quarreling.

<!-- @segment mn48:6.3 -->

What six?

<!-- @segment mn48:6.4 -->

Firstly, a mendicant consistently treats their spiritual companions with bodily kindness, both in public and in private.

<!-- @segment mn48:6.5 -->

This warm-hearted quality makes for fondness and respect, conducing to inclusion, harmony, and unity, without quarreling.

<!-- @segment mn48:6.6 -->

Furthermore, a mendicant consistently treats their spiritual companions with verbal kindness …

<!-- @segment mn48:6.7 -->



<!-- @segment mn48:6.8 -->

Furthermore, a mendicant consistently treats their spiritual companions with mental kindness …

<!-- @segment mn48:6.9 -->



<!-- @segment mn48:6.10 -->

Furthermore, a mendicant shares without reservation any material things they have gained by legitimate means, even the food placed in the alms-bowl, using them in common with their ethical spiritual companions …

<!-- @segment mn48:6.11 -->



<!-- @segment mn48:6.12 -->

Furthermore, a mendicant lives according to the precepts shared with their spiritual companions, both in public and in private. Those precepts are intact, impeccable, spotless, and unmarred, liberating, praised by sensible people, not mistaken, and leading to immersion. …

<!-- @segment mn48:6.13 -->



<!-- @segment mn48:6.14 -->

Furthermore, a mendicant lives according to the view shared with their spiritual companions, both in public and in private. That view is noble and emancipating, and delivers one who practices it to the complete ending of suffering.

<!-- @segment mn48:6.15 -->

This warm-hearted quality makes for fondness and respect, conducing to inclusion, harmony, and unity, without quarreling.

<!-- @segment mn48:6.16 -->

These six warm-hearted qualities make for fondness and respect, conducing to inclusion, harmony, and unity, without quarreling.

<!-- @segment mn48:7.1 -->

Of these six warm-hearted qualities, the chief is the view that is noble and emancipating, and delivers one who practices it to the complete ending of suffering. It holds and binds everything together.

<!-- @segment mn48:7.2 -->

It’s like a bungalow. The roof-peak is the chief point, which holds and binds everything together.

<!-- @segment mn48:7.3 -->

In the same way, of these six warm-hearted qualities, the chief is the view that is noble and emancipating, and delivers one who practices it to the complete ending of suffering. It holds and binds everything together.

<!-- @segment mn48:8.1 -->

And how does the view that is noble and emancipating lead one who practices it to the complete ending of suffering?

<!-- @segment mn48:8.2 -->

It’s when a mendicant has gone to a wilderness, or to the root of a tree, or to an empty hut, and reflects like this,

<!-- @segment mn48:8.3 -->

‘Is there anything that I’m overcome with internally and haven’t given up, because of which I might not accurately know and see?’

<!-- @segment mn48:8.4 -->

If a mendicant is overcome with sensual desire, it’s their mind that’s overcome.

<!-- @segment mn48:8.5 -->

If a mendicant is overcome with ill will,

<!-- @segment mn48:8.6 -->

dullness and drowsiness,

<!-- @segment mn48:8.7 -->

restlessness and remorse,

<!-- @segment mn48:8.8 -->

doubt,

<!-- @segment mn48:8.9 -->

pursuing speculation about this world,

<!-- @segment mn48:8.10 -->

pursuing speculation about the next world,

<!-- @segment mn48:8.11 -->

or fighting, quarreling, and disputing, continually wounding others with barbed words, it’s their mind that’s overcome.

<!-- @segment mn48:8.12 -->

They understand,

<!-- @segment mn48:8.13 -->

‘There is nothing that I’m overcome with internally and haven’t given up, because of which I might not accurately know and see.

<!-- @segment mn48:8.14 -->

My heart is properly disposed for awakening to the truths.’

<!-- @segment mn48:8.15 -->

This is the first knowledge they have achieved that is noble and transcendent, and is distinct from that of ordinary people.

<!-- @segment mn48:9.1 -->

Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects,

<!-- @segment mn48:9.2 -->

‘When I develop, cultivate, and make much of this view, do I personally gain serenity and quenching?’

<!-- @segment mn48:9.3 -->

They understand,

<!-- @segment mn48:9.4 -->

‘When I develop, cultivate, and make much of this view, I personally gain serenity and quenching.’

<!-- @segment mn48:9.5 -->

This is their second knowledge …

<!-- @segment mn48:10.1 -->

Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects,

<!-- @segment mn48:10.2 -->

‘Are there any ascetics or brahmins outside of the Buddhist community who have the same kind of view that I have?’

<!-- @segment mn48:10.3 -->

They understand,

<!-- @segment mn48:10.4 -->

‘There are no ascetics or brahmins outside of the Buddhist community who have the same kind of view that I have.’

<!-- @segment mn48:10.5 -->

This is their third knowledge …

<!-- @segment mn48:11.1 -->

Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects,

<!-- @segment mn48:11.2 -->

‘Do I have the same nature as an individual accomplished in view?’

<!-- @segment mn48:11.3 -->

And what, mendicants, is the nature of an individual accomplished in view?

<!-- @segment mn48:11.4 -->

This is the nature of an individual accomplished in view.

<!-- @segment mn48:11.5 -->

Though they may fall into a kind of offense for which rehabilitation is possible, they quickly disclose, clarify, and reveal it to the Teacher or a sensible spiritual companion.

<!-- @segment mn48:11.6 -->

And having revealed it they restrain themselves in the future.

<!-- @segment mn48:11.7 -->

Suppose there was a little baby boy. If he puts his hand or foot on a burning coal, he quickly pulls it back.

<!-- @segment mn48:11.8 -->

In the same way, this is the nature of an individual accomplished in view.

<!-- @segment mn48:11.9 -->

Though they may still fall into a kind of offense for which rehabilitation is possible, they quickly reveal it to the Teacher or a sensible spiritual companion.

<!-- @segment mn48:11.10 -->

And having revealed it they restrain themselves in the future.

<!-- @segment mn48:11.11 -->

They understand,

<!-- @segment mn48:11.12 -->

‘I have the same nature as an individual accomplished in view.’

<!-- @segment mn48:11.13 -->

This is their fourth knowledge …

<!-- @segment mn48:12.1 -->

Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects,

<!-- @segment mn48:12.2 -->

‘Do I have the same nature as an individual accomplished in view?’

<!-- @segment mn48:12.3 -->

And what, mendicants, is the nature of an individual accomplished in view?

<!-- @segment mn48:12.4 -->

This is the nature of an individual accomplished in view.

<!-- @segment mn48:12.5 -->

Though they might manage a diverse spectrum of duties for their spiritual companions, they still feel a keen regard for the training in higher ethics, higher mind, and higher wisdom.

<!-- @segment mn48:12.6 -->

Suppose there was a cow with a baby calf. She watches her calf devotedly as she grazes.

<!-- @segment mn48:12.7 -->

In the same way, this is the nature of an individual accomplished in view.

<!-- @segment mn48:12.8 -->

Though they might manage a diverse spectrum of duties for their spiritual companions, they still feel a keen regard for the training in higher ethics, higher mind, and higher wisdom.

<!-- @segment mn48:12.9 -->

They understand,

<!-- @segment mn48:12.10 -->

‘I have the same nature as an individual accomplished in view.’

<!-- @segment mn48:12.11 -->

This is their fifth knowledge …

<!-- @segment mn48:13.1 -->

Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects,

<!-- @segment mn48:13.2 -->

‘Do I have the same strength as an individual accomplished in view?’

<!-- @segment mn48:13.3 -->

And what, mendicants, is the strength of an individual accomplished in view?

<!-- @segment mn48:13.4 -->

The strength of an individual accomplished in view is that, when the teaching and training proclaimed by the Realized One are being taught, they pay attention, apply the mind, concentrate wholeheartedly, and actively listen to the teaching.

<!-- @segment mn48:13.5 -->

They understand,

<!-- @segment mn48:13.6 -->

‘I have the same strength as an individual accomplished in view.’

<!-- @segment mn48:13.7 -->

This is their sixth knowledge …

<!-- @segment mn48:14.1 -->

Furthermore, a noble disciple reflects,

<!-- @segment mn48:14.2 -->

‘Do I have the same strength as an individual accomplished in view?’

<!-- @segment mn48:14.3 -->

And what, mendicants, is the strength of an individual accomplished in view?

<!-- @segment mn48:14.4 -->

The strength of an individual accomplished in view is that, when the teaching and training proclaimed by the Realized One are being taught, they find inspiration in the meaning and the teaching, and find joy connected with the teaching.

<!-- @segment mn48:14.5 -->

They understand,

<!-- @segment mn48:14.6 -->

‘I have the same strength as an individual accomplished in view.’

<!-- @segment mn48:14.7 -->

This is the seventh knowledge they have achieved that is noble and transcendent, and is distinct from that of ordinary people.

<!-- @segment mn48:15.1 -->

When a noble disciple has these seven factors, they have properly investigated their nature through the realization of the fruit of stream-entry.

<!-- @segment mn48:15.2 -->

A noble disciple with these seven factors has the fruit of stream-entry.”

<!-- @segment mn48:15.3 -->

That is what the Buddha said.

<!-- @segment mn48:15.4 -->

Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.
