title: The Termite Mound
description: The Buddha expounds a layered parable—revealed to Venerable Kassapa by a deity—of digging into a termite mound that smokes by night and blazes by day, unearthing successive obstacles from sensual craving to ignorance as the way to uproot old defilements.
qualities: examination, wisdom, craving, ignorance, delusion, giving up

<!-- @segment mn23:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment mn23:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s monastery.

<!-- @segment mn23:1.3 -->

Now at that time Venerable Kassapa the Prince was staying in the Dark Forest.

<!-- @segment mn23:1.4 -->

Then, late at night, a glorious deity, lighting up the entire Dark Forest, went up to Kassapa the Prince, stood to one side, and said:

<!-- @segment mn23:2.1 -->

“Monk, monk! This termite mound fumes by night and flames by day.

<!-- @segment mn23:2.2 -->

The brahmin said,

<!-- @segment mn23:2.3 -->

‘Dig, clever one, having picked up the sword!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.4 -->

Picking up the sword and digging, the clever one saw a sticking point:

<!-- @segment mn23:2.5 -->

‘A sticking point, sir!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.6 -->

The brahmin said,

<!-- @segment mn23:2.7 -->

‘Throw out the sticking point!

<!-- @segment mn23:2.8 -->

Dig, clever one, having picked up the sword!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.9 -->

Picking up the sword and digging, the clever one saw a bullfrog:

<!-- @segment mn23:2.10 -->

‘A bullfrog, sir!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.11 -->

The brahmin said,

<!-- @segment mn23:2.12 -->

‘Throw out the bullfrog!

<!-- @segment mn23:2.13 -->

Dig, clever one, having picked up the sword!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.14 -->

Picking up the sword and digging, the clever one saw a forked path:

<!-- @segment mn23:2.15 -->

‘A forked path, sir!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.16 -->

The brahmin said,

<!-- @segment mn23:2.17 -->

‘Throw out the forked path!

<!-- @segment mn23:2.18 -->

Dig, clever one, having picked up the sword!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.19 -->

Picking up the sword and digging, the clever one saw a filter of ash:

<!-- @segment mn23:2.20 -->

‘A filter of ash, sir!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.21 -->

The brahmin said,

<!-- @segment mn23:2.22 -->

‘Throw out the filter of ash!

<!-- @segment mn23:2.23 -->

Dig, clever one, having picked up the sword!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.24 -->

Picking up the sword and digging, the clever one saw a tortoise:

<!-- @segment mn23:2.25 -->

‘A tortoise, sir!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.26 -->

The brahmin said,

<!-- @segment mn23:2.27 -->

‘Throw out the tortoise!

<!-- @segment mn23:2.28 -->

Dig, clever one, having taken up the sword!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.29 -->

Picking up the sword and digging, the clever one saw a butcher’s knife and chopping board:

<!-- @segment mn23:2.30 -->

‘A butcher’s knife and chopping board, sir!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.31 -->

The brahmin said,

<!-- @segment mn23:2.32 -->

‘Throw out the butcher’s knife and chopping board!

<!-- @segment mn23:2.33 -->

Dig, clever one, having picked up the sword!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.34 -->

Picking up the sword and digging, the clever one saw a scrap of meat:

<!-- @segment mn23:2.35 -->

‘A scrap of meat, sir!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.36 -->

The brahmin said,

<!-- @segment mn23:2.37 -->

‘Throw out the scrap of meat!

<!-- @segment mn23:2.38 -->

Dig, clever one, having picked up the sword!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.39 -->

Picking up the sword and digging, the clever one saw a cobra:

<!-- @segment mn23:2.40 -->

‘A cobra, sir!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.41 -->

The brahmin said,

<!-- @segment mn23:2.42 -->

‘Leave the cobra! Do not disturb the cobra! Worship the cobra!’

<!-- @segment mn23:2.43 -->

Mendicant, go to the Buddha and ask him about this riddle. You should remember it in line with his answer.

<!-- @segment mn23:2.44 -->

I don’t see anyone in this world—with its gods, Māras, and Divinities, this population with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans—who could provide a satisfying answer to this riddle except for the Realized One or his disciple or someone who has heard it from them.”

<!-- @segment mn23:2.45 -->

That is what that deity said

<!-- @segment mn23:2.46 -->

before vanishing right there.

<!-- @segment mn23:3.1 -->

Then, when the night had passed, Kassapa the Prince went to the Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him what had happened. Then he asked:

<!-- @segment mn23:3.2 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:3.3 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:3.4 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:3.5 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:3.6 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:3.7 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:3.8 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:3.9 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:3.10 -->

“Sir, what is the termite mound? What is the fuming by night and flaming by day? Who is the brahmin, and who the clever one? What are the sword, the digging, the sticking point, the bullfrog, the forked path, the filter of ash, the tortoise, the butcher’s knife and chopping board, and the scrap of meat? And what is the cobra?”

<!-- @segment mn23:4.1 -->

“Mendicant, ‘termite mound’ is a term for this body made up of the four principal states, produced by mother and father, built up from rice and porridge, liable to impermanence, to wearing away and erosion, to breaking up and destruction.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.2 -->

Thinking and considering all night about what you did during the day—

<!-- @segment mn23:4.3 -->

this is the fuming at night.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.4 -->

The work you apply yourself to during the day by body, speech, and mind after thinking about it all night—

<!-- @segment mn23:4.5 -->

this is the flaming by day.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.6 -->

‘Brahmin’ is a term for the Realized One, the perfected one, the fully awakened Buddha.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.7 -->

‘Clever one’ is a term for the trainee mendicant.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.8 -->

‘Sword’ is a term for noble wisdom.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.9 -->

‘Digging’ is a term for rousing energy.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.10 -->

‘Sticking point’ is a term for ignorance.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.11 -->

‘Throw out the sticking point’ means ‘give up ignorance,

<!-- @segment mn23:4.12 -->

dig, clever one, having picked up the sword.’

<!-- @segment mn23:4.13 -->

‘Bullfrog’ is a term for anger and distress.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.14 -->

‘Throw out the bullfrog’ means ‘give up anger and distress’ …

<!-- @segment mn23:4.15 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:4.16 -->

‘A forked path’ is a term for doubt.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.17 -->

‘Throw out the forked path’ means ‘give up doubt’ …

<!-- @segment mn23:4.18 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:4.19 -->

‘A filter of ash’ is a term for the five hindrances, that is:

<!-- @segment mn23:4.20 -->

the hindrances of sensual desire, ill will, dullness and drowsiness, restlessness and remorse, and doubt.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.21 -->

‘Throw out the filter of ash’ means ‘give up the five hindrances’ …

<!-- @segment mn23:4.22 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:4.23 -->

‘Tortoise’ is a term for the five grasping aggregates, that is:

<!-- @segment mn23:4.24 -->

form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.25 -->

‘Throw out the tortoise’ means ‘give up the five grasping aggregates’ …

<!-- @segment mn23:4.26 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:4.27 -->

‘Butcher’s knife and chopping board’ is a term for the five kinds of sensual stimulation.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.28 -->

Sights known by the eye, which are likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.29 -->

Sounds known by the ear …

<!-- @segment mn23:4.30 -->

Smells known by the nose …

<!-- @segment mn23:4.31 -->

Tastes known by the tongue …

<!-- @segment mn23:4.32 -->

Touches known by the body, which are likable, desirable, agreeable, pleasant, sensual, and arousing.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.33 -->

‘Throw out the butcher’s knife and chopping board’ means ‘give up the five kinds of sensual stimulation’ …

<!-- @segment mn23:4.34 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:4.35 -->

‘Scrap of meat’ is a term for greed and relishing.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.36 -->

‘Throw out the scrap of meat’ means ‘give up greed and relishing’ …

<!-- @segment mn23:4.37 -->



<!-- @segment mn23:4.38 -->

‘Cobra’ is a term for a mendicant who has ended the defilements.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.39 -->

This is the meaning of: ‘Leave the cobra! Do not disturb the cobra! Worship the cobra!’”

<!-- @segment mn23:4.40 -->

That is what the Buddha said.

<!-- @segment mn23:4.41 -->

Satisfied, Venerable Kassapa the Prince approved what the Buddha said.
