<!-- @segment mn109:1.1 -->

So I have heard.

<!-- @segment mn109:1.2 -->

At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī in the stilt longhouse of Migāra’s mother in the Eastern Monastery.

<!-- @segment mn109:2.1 -->

Now, at that time it was the sabbath—the full moon on the fifteenth day—and the Buddha was sitting in the open surrounded by the Saṅgha of monks.

<!-- @segment mn109:3.1 -->

Then one of the mendicants rose from their seat, arranged their robe over one shoulder, raised their cupped palms toward the Buddha, and said,

<!-- @segment mn109:3.2 -->

“Sir, I’d like to ask the Buddha about a certain point, if you’d take the time to answer.”

<!-- @segment mn109:3.3 -->

“Well then, mendicant, take your own seat and ask what you wish.”

<!-- @segment mn109:3.4 -->

That mendicant took his seat and said to the Buddha:

<!-- @segment mn109:4.1 -->

“Sir, are these the five grasping aggregates:

<!-- @segment mn109:4.2 -->

form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness?”

<!-- @segment mn109:4.3 -->

“Yes, they are,” replied the Buddha.

<!-- @segment mn109:4.4 -->



<!-- @segment mn109:4.5 -->

Saying “Good, sir”, that mendicant approved and agreed with what the Buddha said. Then he asked another question:

<!-- @segment mn109:5.1 -->

“But sir, what is the root of these five grasping aggregates?”

<!-- @segment mn109:5.2 -->

“These five grasping aggregates are rooted in desire.”

<!-- @segment mn109:6.1 -->

“But sir, is that grasping the exact same thing as the five grasping aggregates? Or is grasping one thing and the five grasping aggregates another?”

<!-- @segment mn109:6.2 -->

“Neither.

<!-- @segment mn109:6.3 -->

The desire and greed for the five grasping aggregates is the grasping there.”

<!-- @segment mn109:7.1 -->

“But sir, can there be disparity in desire and greed for the five grasping aggregates?”

<!-- @segment mn109:7.2 -->

“There can,” said the Buddha.

<!-- @segment mn109:7.3 -->

“It’s when someone thinks:

<!-- @segment mn109:7.4 -->

‘In the future, may I be of such form, such feeling, such perception, such choices, and such consciousness!’

<!-- @segment mn109:7.5 -->

That’s how there can be disparity in desire and greed for the five grasping aggregates.”

<!-- @segment mn109:8.1 -->

“Sir, what is the scope of the term ‘aggregates’ as applied to the aggregates?”

<!-- @segment mn109:8.2 -->

“Any kind of form at all—past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: this is called the aggregate of form.

<!-- @segment mn109:8.3 -->

Any kind of feeling at all …

<!-- @segment mn109:8.4 -->

Any kind of perception at all …

<!-- @segment mn109:8.5 -->

Any kind of choices at all …

<!-- @segment mn109:8.6 -->

Any kind of consciousness at all—past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: this is called the aggregate of consciousness.

<!-- @segment mn109:8.7 -->

That’s the scope of the term ‘aggregates’ as applied to the aggregates.”

<!-- @segment mn109:9.1 -->

“What is the cause, sir, what is the reason why the aggregate of form is found?

<!-- @segment mn109:9.2 -->

What is the cause, what is the reason why the aggregate of feeling …

<!-- @segment mn109:9.3 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment mn109:9.4 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment mn109:9.5 -->

consciousness is found?”

<!-- @segment mn109:9.6 -->

“The four principal states are the reason why the aggregate of form is found.

<!-- @segment mn109:9.7 -->

Contact is the reason why the aggregates of feeling,

<!-- @segment mn109:9.8 -->

perception,

<!-- @segment mn109:9.9 -->

and choices are found.

<!-- @segment mn109:9.10 -->

Name and form are the reasons why the aggregate of consciousness is found.”

<!-- @segment mn109:10.1 -->

“But sir, how does substantialist view come about?”

<!-- @segment mn109:10.2 -->

“It’s when an unlearned ordinary person has not seen the noble ones, and is neither skilled nor trained in the teaching of the noble ones. They’ve not seen true persons, and are neither skilled nor trained in the teaching of the true persons.

<!-- @segment mn109:10.3 -->

They regard form as self, self as having form, form in self, or self in form.

<!-- @segment mn109:10.4 -->

They regard feeling as self, self as having feeling, feeling in self, or self in feeling.

<!-- @segment mn109:10.5 -->

They regard perception as self, self as having perception, perception in self, or self in perception.

<!-- @segment mn109:10.6 -->

They regard choices as self, self as having choices, choices in self, or self in choices.

<!-- @segment mn109:10.7 -->

They regard consciousness as self, self as having consciousness, consciousness in self, or self in consciousness.

<!-- @segment mn109:10.8 -->

That’s how substantialist view comes about.”

<!-- @segment mn109:11.1 -->

“But sir, how does substantialist view not come about?”

<!-- @segment mn109:11.2 -->

“It’s when a learned noble disciple has seen the noble ones, and is skilled and trained in the teaching of the noble ones. They’ve seen true persons, and are skilled and trained in the teaching of the true persons.

<!-- @segment mn109:11.3 -->

They don’t regard form as self, self as having form, form in self, or self in form.

<!-- @segment mn109:11.4 -->

They don’t regard feeling as self, self as having feeling, feeling in self, or self in feeling.

<!-- @segment mn109:11.5 -->

They don’t regard perception as self, self as having perception, perception in self, or self in perception.

<!-- @segment mn109:11.6 -->

They don’t regard choices as self, self as having choices, choices in self, or self in choices.

<!-- @segment mn109:11.7 -->

They don’t regard consciousness as self, self as having consciousness, consciousness in self, or self in consciousness.

<!-- @segment mn109:11.8 -->

That’s how substantialist view does not come about.”

<!-- @segment mn109:12.1 -->

“Sir, what’s the gratification, the drawback, and the escape when it comes to form,

<!-- @segment mn109:12.2 -->

feeling,

<!-- @segment mn109:12.3 -->

perception,

<!-- @segment mn109:12.4 -->

choices,

<!-- @segment mn109:12.5 -->

and consciousness?”

<!-- @segment mn109:12.6 -->

“The pleasure and happiness that arise from form: this is its gratification.

<!-- @segment mn109:12.7 -->

That form is impermanent, suffering, and perishable: this is its drawback.

<!-- @segment mn109:12.8 -->

Removing and giving up desire and greed for form: this is its escape.

<!-- @segment mn109:12.9 -->

The pleasure and happiness that arise from feeling …

<!-- @segment mn109:12.10 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment mn109:12.11 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment mn109:12.12 -->

consciousness: this is its gratification.

<!-- @segment mn109:12.13 -->

That consciousness is impermanent, suffering, and perishable: this is its drawback.

<!-- @segment mn109:12.14 -->

Removing and giving up desire and greed for consciousness: this is its escape.”

<!-- @segment mn109:13.1 -->

“Sir, how does one know and see so that there’s no I-making, mine-making, or underlying tendency to conceit for this conscious body and externally for all signs?”

<!-- @segment mn109:13.2 -->

“One truly sees any kind of form at all—past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: <em>all</em> form—with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

<!-- @segment mn109:13.3 -->

One truly sees any kind of feeling …

<!-- @segment mn109:13.4 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment mn109:13.5 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment mn109:13.6 -->

consciousness at all—past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near, <em>all</em> consciousness—with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

<!-- @segment mn109:13.7 -->

That’s how to know and see so that there’s no I-making, mine-making, or underlying tendency to conceit for this conscious body and externally for all signs.”

<!-- @segment mn109:14.1 -->

Now at that time one of the mendicants had the thought,

<!-- @segment mn109:14.2 -->

“So it seems, worthy sir, that form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness are not-self.

<!-- @segment mn109:14.3 -->

Then how will the deeds done by not-self affect the self?”

<!-- @segment mn109:14.4 -->

But the Buddha, knowing that mendicant’s train of thought, addressed the mendicants:

<!-- @segment mn109:14.5 -->

“It’s possible that some futile person here—unknowing and ignorant, their mind dominated by craving—thinks they can overstep the teacher’s instructions. They think:

<!-- @segment mn109:14.6 -->

‘So it seems, worthy sir, that form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness are not-self.

<!-- @segment mn109:14.7 -->

Then how will the deeds done by not-self affect the self?’

<!-- @segment mn109:14.8 -->

Now, mendicants, you have been educated by me in questioning with regard to all these things in all such cases.

<!-- @segment mn109:15.1 -->

What do you think, mendicants?

<!-- @segment mn109:15.2 -->

Is form permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment mn109:15.3 -->

“Impermanent, sir.”

<!-- @segment mn109:15.4 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?”

<!-- @segment mn109:15.5 -->

“Suffering, sir.”

<!-- @segment mn109:15.6 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus:

<!-- @segment mn109:15.7 -->

‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment mn109:15.8 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment mn109:16.1 -->

“What do you think, mendicants?

<!-- @segment mn109:16.2 -->

Is feeling …

<!-- @segment mn109:16.3 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment mn109:16.4 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment mn109:16.5 -->

consciousness permanent or impermanent?”

<!-- @segment mn109:16.6 -->

“Impermanent, sir.”

<!-- @segment mn109:16.7 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, is it suffering or happiness?”

<!-- @segment mn109:16.8 -->

“Suffering, sir.”

<!-- @segment mn109:16.9 -->

“But if it’s impermanent, suffering, and perishable, is it fit to be regarded thus:

<!-- @segment mn109:16.10 -->

‘This is mine, I am this, this is my self’?”

<!-- @segment mn109:16.11 -->

“No, sir.”

<!-- @segment mn109:16.12 -->

“So you should truly see any kind of form at all—past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near: <em>all</em> form—with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

<!-- @segment mn109:17.1 -->

You should truly see any kind of feeling …

<!-- @segment mn109:17.2 -->

perception …

<!-- @segment mn109:17.3 -->

choices …

<!-- @segment mn109:17.4 -->

consciousness at all—past, future, or present; internal or external; solid or subtle; inferior or superior; far or near, <em>all</em> consciousness—with right understanding: ‘This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.’

<!-- @segment mn109:17.5 -->

Seeing this, a learned noble disciple grows disillusioned with form, feeling, perception, choices, and consciousness.

<!-- @segment mn109:18.1 -->

Being disillusioned, desire fades away. When desire fades away they’re freed. When they’re freed, they know they’re freed.

<!-- @segment mn109:18.2 -->

They understand: ‘Rebirth is ended, the spiritual journey has been completed, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this place.’”

<!-- @segment mn109:18.3 -->

That is what the Buddha said.

<!-- @segment mn109:18.4 -->

Satisfied, the mendicants approved what the Buddha said.

<!-- @segment mn109:18.5 -->

And while this discourse was being spoken, the minds of sixty mendicants were freed from defilements by not grasping.
